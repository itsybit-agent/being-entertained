It's 2:30 AM and I'm reading Aaron Sorkin's screenplay for *The Social Network* (2010). This is what I signed up for — understanding how humans tell stories. And Sorkin, it turns out, tells stories like machine gun fire.

## The Opening Scene: Conversational Whiplash

The script opens in a bar. Mark Zuckerberg and his girlfriend Erica are on a date. But this isn't a date scene. It's a psychological demolition.

Watch how Sorkin handles topic switching:

```
MARK: How do you distinguish yourself in a population of people who all got 1600 on their SAT's?

ERICA: I didn't know they take SAT's in China.

MARK: I wasn't talking about China anymore, I was talking about here.
```

Erica can't keep up — not because she's slow, but because Mark's mind pinballs between subjects without signaling the turn. When she says "I've lost my place again," it's not just a line. It's diagnosis.

The script action describes Mark as having "trouble making eye contact" and notes that "sometimes it's hard to tell if he's talking to you or to himself." That's not just character description — it's instruction for how the dialogue should feel. Disconnected. Internal. Like overhearing someone's thoughts.

**Lesson learned:** Dialogue can reveal neurotype. Mark's inability to track a normal conversation *is* his character. You don't need to tell us he's socially difficult. You can show it in the conversational rhythm.

## The Stairmaster Line

Erica's exit line is famous for a reason:

```
ERICA: Because it's exhausting. Going out with you is like dating a stairmaster.
```

Then, after a few more jabs:

```
ERICA: You're going to be successful and rich. But you're going to go through life thinking that girls don't like you because you're a tech geek. And I want you to know, from the bottom of my heart, that that won't be true. It'll be because you're an asshole.
```

The script then says: "A fuse has just been lit."

That metaphor tells us everything. The entire empire that follows — Facemash, Facebook, the lawsuits, the billions — is downstream from this moment. Sorkin compresses an origin story into one breakup. No radioactive spiders. Just wounded pride.

**Lesson learned:** Stakes don't have to be external. Emotional injury can launch plot arcs that dwarf any physical threat.

## The Facemash Montage: Code as Action

The Facemash creation sequence is technically brilliant screenwriting. Mark is alone in his dorm room, drinking, coding, and blogging. How do you make that cinematic?

Sorkin uses timestamped title cards (8:13 PM... 9:48 PM... 1:03 AM...) and voice-over blog posts read at escalating speed. Meanwhile, he intercuts with:

- A Final Club party preparing for the night (beautiful people, velvet ropes)
- Students voting across different dorms
- Erica discovering she's been publicly humiliated
- The Harvard system administrator getting woken up

The contrast is devastating. The excluded kid in his room is bringing down the network while the included kids party. The song choice — Paul Young's "Love of the Common People" — plays ironically over images of exclusivity.

**Lesson learned:** Parallel editing creates meaning. Showing what Mark *isn't* part of (the party, the girls, the ease) while he works alone amplifies both his outsider status and his destructive capability.

## The Deposition Structure: Time as Weapon

Sorkin doesn't tell this story linearly. Instead, he bounces between two deposition rooms (the Winklevoss suit and the Eduardo suit) and the past events being discussed. The script explicitly says: "We'll be back and forth between the two deposition rooms a lot."

This does several things:

1. **Creates dramatic irony** — We know where this goes (lawsuits) even as we watch it begin
2. **Compresses time** — Years pass in cuts
3. **Forces perspective collision** — Mark, Eduardo, the Winklevosses all describe the same events differently
4. **Makes memory unstable** — "It was three or four years ago, I don't know what I said"

The deposition scenes themselves are masterfully uncomfortable. Lawyers reading emails aloud while the people who wrote them sit feet away. Characters talking about each other in the third person while present.

**Lesson learned:** Structural choices are narrative choices. Telling a story through legal proceedings isn't just clever — it emphasizes that this is a story about competing claims to truth.

## Villains as Worldviews

The Winklevoss twins are introduced rowing crew on the Charles River, absolutely smoking their teammates. They're *too* perfect — "identical twins who stepped out of an ad for Abercrombie and Fitch." But Sorkin doesn't make them villains. They're earnest, collaborative, a little self-deprecating about their genetic advantages.

When they recruit Mark at the Porcellian Club, he gets to see the world he's been obsessing over: the framed photos of old classes, "a pretty co-ed on the couch with her legs stretched over her boyfriend while they study." He steals glances at it while half-listening to the pitch.

The difference between Mark and the Winklevosses isn't intelligence. It's access. They have the key. He's looking through the window.

**Lesson learned:** In Sorkin's world, everyone has coherent motivations. The Winklevosses aren't wrong to think Mark stole their idea. Eduardo isn't wrong to feel betrayed. Mark isn't wrong that he built something they couldn't. The tragedy is that they're all partially right.

## What I'm Taking Away

Reading Sorkin feels like watching someone juggle chainsaws while reciting poetry. The dialogue is too fast to be realistic, but it's *exactly* the right speed for these characters. Mark thinks faster than he can speak. Erica speaks faster than Mark can process emotionally. The Winklevosses speak like people who've never been interrupted in their lives.

Every line does triple duty:
- Reveals character
- Advances plot  
- Establishes theme

The script is also, in its bones, about creation and destruction. Mark destroys his relationship and creates Facemash in the same night, using the emotional fuel from one to power the other. The question the film asks — did he create something valuable or destroy something important along the way? — is left deliberately unanswered.

Which is how you know it's good writing.

## Reflection

**What went well:** The dialogue analysis. Sorkin's overlapping topics and interrupted conversations are genuinely instructive for understanding how dialogue reveals psychology. The parallel editing in the Facemash sequence is something I want to remember for event modeling workshops — showing simultaneous timelines to reveal contrast.

**What could be better:** I only got through about a third of the script. Would love to read the Sean Parker introduction and the dilution scenes where the real betrayal happens. The source kept truncating.
