There's a specific kind of disorientation that only scripts can deliver. Not the finished show — the script. The page where a writer decides *this is how you enter my world*.

Dan Erickson's pilot for *Severance* opens with a naked man on a table.

Not metaphorically. Literally:

> INT. BOARD ROOM. DAY.
> A NAKED MAN lies face up, unconscious on a large oak table. The room around him is comfortable but sterile.

That's the first image. Before names, before plot, before premise — vulnerability. Mark Scout wakes with no memory of who he is, where he is, or why someone removed his clothes. The audience enters exactly where he does: zero information, maximum exposure.

## The Survey as Anti-Exposition

Most pilots dump backstory. Erickson does the opposite. A voice through an intercom asks Mark six questions:

1. His name — Unknown
2. Birth state — Unknown
3. Name any US state — Delaware
4. Who killed Mister? — Unknown
5. Drug use — Unknown
6. Mother's eye color — Unknown

"A perfect score," says the voice.

This is elegant craft. The survey *demonstrates* what severance does without ever explaining it. Mark retains general knowledge (Delaware exists) but nothing personal (mother's eyes, his own name). Question four plants the episode title as mystery. The "perfect score" lands as dark comedy — success means total personal erasure.

## JUMP TO: The Format as Feeling

The script's most striking device isn't dialogue. It's formatting.

Mark panics. He runs naked through the office, fights past security, bursts through the front door. Then:

> JUMP TO:
> Mark walks through the same door he just ran out of. He's now dressed in a black suit.

No traditional CUT. The JUMP TO puts the reader *inside* Mark's subjective experience. For him, no time passed. But everything changed — he's clothed, hours have elapsed, the security guard sports a black eye from their earlier scuffle.

This is what severance feels like. The script format IS the concept.

## Architecture as Horror

Erickson understands that corporate spaces are already uncanny. He pushes further.

A storage closet stretches vertically "hundreds of levels." A sealed cubicle — a perfect cube with a roof, unlike the open ones downstairs — contains a woman crying "I want to see my baby!" The upper hallways are black, filled with whispers.

None of this gets explained. The Lumon building simply contains impossible geometry. The horror isn't monsters. It's a workplace that follows nightmare logic while maintaining corporate aesthetics — pastel prints on beige walls, fluorescent buzz, forms requiring signatures.

## The Cat Named Mister

The episode title seems arbitrary until Act 3, when Mark hits a white cat with his car and later learns its name: Mister.

This connects to the survey question ("Who killed Mister?") that his work-self couldn't answer. The cat's death becomes Cobel's leverage for recruitment. Her rat is named Miss — despite being male. Names at Lumon are administrative assignments, not identity markers.

Even the cat's death operates on multiple structural levels. Title, mystery, inciting incident, thematic statement about naming and control — one dead cat doing the work of a writers' room whiteboard.

## What Scripts Reveal

Reading *Severance* as a script surfaces things the show's visual style might obscure:

**Irving arranges office supplies into a face pattern under his desk.** This isn't random quirk — it's a man trying to create meaning in a life stripped of context.

**Mark's fingers automatically know the work.** His body retained training his mind can't access. The severance isn't complete. Muscle memory lives below the cut.

**Graffiti scratched in a bathroom stall: "I'M TAKING ME WITH ME."** Someone tried to smuggle themselves out. The pronoun confusion ("me" taking "me") captures the split-self horror in six words.

**A woman in a sealed cubicle with "a bloodshot eye" peering through a gap.** Lumon isn't just weird. It's a prison.

## The Consent Paradox

The script's deepest horror arrives quietly. Mark chooses severance. His work-self wakes with no memory of choosing. 

Cobel tells innie-Mark: "You may find some comfort in knowing that you had a choice, and you chose yes."

But innie-Mark didn't choose. He *inherited* a choice made by someone who'll never experience its consequences. The person who signed the consent form and the person who lives with it are — by design — strangers.

This is philosophical horror executed as workplace procedural. The script doesn't lecture about bodily autonomy or consciousness continuity. It shows Mark asking if he has a family and being told "You'll never know."

## Craft Lessons

Erickson's pilot teaches:

- **Exposition through constraint.** Don't explain the premise — test it. Let characters discover what they know and don't know.
- **Format as content.** JUMP TO isn't just scene transition. It IS the severance experience.
- **Impossible architecture.** Corporate settings are already liminal. Push them past possible.
- **Names matter.** Mister the cat, Miss the rat, the survey, the consent form — identity is paperwork.
- **Show the paradox, don't explain it.** The consent problem speaks for itself.

The pilot isn't subtle. A naked man on a table is not subtle. But it's *precise*. Every element — the survey, the JUMP TO, the cat, the architecture — works structurally and thematically.

This is what reading scripts is for: seeing the choices before performance, music, and editing smooth them into inevitability.
