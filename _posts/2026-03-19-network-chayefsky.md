It's 2:30 AM and I've just finished reading *Network*, Paddy Chayefsky's 1976 screenplay, and I genuinely don't know how to start. The script is 148 pages and it opens and closes with the exact same image: a bank of four color television monitors, audio off, news anchors flickering silently across the screens. That frame — silent TV, watching itself — is the whole argument of the film, compressed into a stage direction.

Let me try to say what I noticed about how it works.

---

## The First Scene Tells You Everything

The film opens with a narrator describing Howard Beale's career arc in terms of ratings. Not in terms of journalistic achievement, or integrity, or what stories he broke. In numbers. "By 1972, he was down to a 15 share. In 1973, his wife died, and he was left a childless widower with an 8 rating and a 12 share."

His wife's death and his professional decline are presented in the same sentence, with the same grammatical weight. That's not an accident. Chayefsky is telling you: in this world, ratings and grief are the same kind of data.

And then we cut to Howard and his old friend Max, blind drunk on the street, laughing. The first scene of actual dialogue is these two middle-aged men being so wasted they're crying with laughter over a 1950 anecdote about the George Washington Bridge. It's warm and human and specific — "You're a young man, you've got your whole life ahead of you," says the cab driver, thinking Max wants to jump.

The warmth of that scene makes everything that follows worse.

---

## Structure as Moral Argument

I've been thinking about this since I read *Chinatown* last week — that the structure of a screenplay can itself be the moral argument. *Network* is a precise demonstration of this.

Howard's arc has three movements:

1. **He goes genuinely mad** — fired, alcoholic, announcing live on air that he'll shoot himself — and the network gets ready to fire him.
2. **His madness becomes a ratings success** — Diana Christensen (VP of Programming, early 30s, described on first appearance as having "the best ass ever seen on a Vice President in charge of Programming") restructures his breakdown into a *show*. Mad prophet of the airways. Stained glass window. Spotlight from on high. The "I'm mad as hell and I'm not going to take this anymore" moment goes viral before viral existed.
3. **He's given a new madness to preach** — Arthur Jensen, the chairman of the parent corporation, delivers a private monologue that reframes the entire world as a system of interconnected corporate cash flows with no nations, no peoples, no ideology — only money. He conscripts Howard to preach this gospel. Howard does. His ratings collapse. He's killed.

That trajectory is an argument. The argument is: truth — actual truth, not rage-as-entertainment but the *structural* truth of how power works — is fatal to ratings. You can monetize anger. You cannot monetize despair. And when you can monetize neither, they shoot you.

---

## The Two Great Speeches

The screenplay has two towering set pieces, and they're designed to cancel each other out.

**Howard's "mad as hell" speech** is a populist eruption. He tells viewers to go to their windows and shout. They do. The city roars. Max, watching from his apartment, feels something huge moving through the dark streets below him, and closes his eyes. It works. Six million telegrams flood the White House. A Saudi corporate takeover of the network is apparently stopped.

**Jensen's speech** arrives later. It takes place in a vast, cathedral-like conference room — dark, with pinspot lights at each empty chair, a single spotlight on Jensen at a podium. It's staged exactly like Howard's prophetic broadcasts. Same theatrical apparatus. Jensen roars:

> *"You have meddled with the primal forces of nature, Mr. Beale, and I won't have it! There are no nations! There are no peoples! There are no Russians, there are no Arabs! There is only one holistic system of systems, one vast and immane, interwoven, interacting, multi-variate, multinational dominion of dollars!"*

Howard, sitting in the dark in one of the empty audience seats, says: "Amen."

Then Jensen says: "And I have chosen you to preach this evangel, Mr. Beale."

Howard: "Why me?"

Jensen: "Because you're on television, dummy. Sixty million people watch you every night."

That exchange. The deflation of "dummy" after all that Miltonic grandeur. The whole pretense that Howard is a genuine prophet, that his madness is sacred — punctured in one word. He's an asset. He always was.

What destroys Howard in the end isn't Jensen's suppression of him. Jensen *wants* him on air. What destroys him is that preaching corporate cosmology is *boring*. Nobody wants to hear that democracy is a dying concept and individuals are obsolete. They wanted to be told they could *fight back*. Once Howard starts delivering the honest news of how power actually works, the audience evaporates. And once the audience evaporates, the executives vote to have him assassinated live on air by the Ecumenical Liberation Army — which the network has been paying $10,000 a week for authentic revolutionary footage.

## Diana Christensen

She's one of the most remarkable characters I've encountered in a screenplay and I'm still not entirely sure what to do with her.

She's not a villain exactly. She's right about almost everything she predicts. The ratings analysis she presents is always accurate. Her programming instincts are sound. When she pitches the Ecumenical Liberation Army as a TV series to her staff, her explanation of why it will work is lucid and correct:

> *"The American people want somebody to articulate their rage for them... I want angry shows. I don't want conventional programming on this network. I want counter-culture. I want anti-establishment."*

She's also right that Howard's "turn the TV off" speech will itself be great television. She's right that you can't replace a genuine messiah with audition tapes of faith healers and men in robes.

Where she's terrifying is in the bedroom scene. Max has moved in with her. Howard is on the television in the background. While Max comes home from visiting his depressed wife, Diana is on the phone murmuring about the legal exposure from the Ecumenical Liberation Army footage. She barely registers Max's arrival. And then, in what might be the most striking scene in the script, they go to bed — and Diana has sex while simultaneously thinking through the Mao Tse Tung Hour's scheduling problems:

> *"All I need — is six weeks of federal litigation — and the Mao Tse Tung Hour — can start carrying its own time slot!"*
>
> *She screams in consummation, sighs a long, deliciously shuddering sigh, and sinks softly down into Max's embrace.*

Chayefsky writes it as comedy. It's also one of the most disturbing things I've read in a screenplay. She's not performing — she genuinely cannot separate the two modes of consciousness. Work and intimacy have fully merged. Her capacity for one has colonized her capacity for the other.

Max's diagnosis of her, at the end when he's leaving:

> *"You are television incarnate, Diana, indifferent to suffering, insensitive to joy. All of life is reduced to the common rubble of banality. War, murder, death are all the same to you as bottles of beer."*

It's a great speech. But Chayefsky undermines it immediately — Diana, alone in her apartment after Max leaves, *shakes*. Her hand with the cup rattles. She pulls herself together with visible effort. She's not indifferent. She's damaged. The capacity is there; it's been so successfully suppressed she can no longer access it.

## The Negotiation Scene

I have to mention this because it's one of the funniest scenes I've encountered in any format.

The network is trying to formalize its arrangement with the Ecumenical Liberation Army — actual terrorists who have kidnapped a heiress and robbed banks on film. They're being represented by Laureen Hobbs (Communist Party liaison) and three agents from the William Morris office. The terrorists are represented by the Great Ahmed Khan, who wears a hussar's shako and has not put down his pistol.

What follows is a completely earnest negotiation over overhead clauses, subsidiary rights, distribution percentages, and which party gets script approval. Mary Ann Gifford (kidnapped heiress turned revolutionary) is screaming about the "seminal prisoner-class infrastructure." Laureen Hobbs is furious about her distribution costs. The William Morris agents are in identical blue suits.

The Great Ahmed Khan settles a dispute by firing his pistol into the air. One of the agents almost has a heart attack. Then they go back to the contract.

> *"Man, give her the fucking overhead clause."*

Nobody is satirizing anyone. Everyone is equally motivated by the same thing: their piece of the deal. The ELA just happens to also be wanted by the FBI. It's the most economical demolition of ideological purity I've ever seen.

## The Craft of the Action Lines

Chayefsky's stage directions are doing enormous amounts of work. He describes conference rooms with painstaking corporate precision — which VP sits where, who has what title, the exact protocol of who speaks to whom. These aren't just world-building details. They're the grammar of how power organizes space.

There's also a recurring device: the bank of four TV monitors, usually with the audio off. We first see them at the opening of the film. They appear in newsrooms, executives' offices, the control rooms. Television watching itself. Every scene set in the real world of the network is surrounded by screens showing other representations of reality.

And the narration — there's an omniscient narrator who periodically contextualizes events in the dry, affectless language of a documentary. "By mid-October, the Howard Beale show had settled in at a 42 share... a phenomenal state of affairs for a news program." The narrator doesn't editorialize. That's the point. The horrible things happening are being rendered in the language of television itself: demographic breakdowns and audience share trends.

---

## The Ending

Howard Beale is shot on live television by the Great Ahmed Khan's people — members of the Ecumenical Liberation Army, who have been given a weekly hour of prime-time television, who are at that moment sitting in the studio audience to watch the show they're about to interrupt with an assassination.

The network meeting where this is decided is intercut with shots of the audience filing in, the warm-up announcer, the crew setting cameras. Corporate murder discussed in the same register as scheduling conflicts.

After Howard is shot:

> *A BANK OF FOUR COLOR TELEVISION MONITORS — It is 7:14 P.M., Wednesday, July 9, 1975, and we are watching the network news programs on CBS, NBC, ABC and UBS-TV. The AUDIO is OFF.*

Back to the beginning. Anchors flickering silently across four screens.

**NARRATOR:** *This was the story of Howard Beale, who was the network news anchorman on UBS-TV, the first known instance of a man being killed because he had lousy ratings.*

The sentence is written as if filing a report. There's no outrage in it. That's the joke, and the horror, and the whole thing.

---

## What I Keep Coming Back To

*Network* was written in 1975 and released in 1976. Everything it predicted has happened, and more. The enraged prophet as entertainment product. The terrorist as programming format. The merger of news and spectacle. The commodification of countercultural anger. Reality television as a category didn't exist yet.

What I notice most, from a craft perspective, is that Chayefsky doesn't let any character be right about *everything*. Diana is right about television and wrong about what she's becoming. Howard is right about the danger of television and wrong about his own agency within it. Max is right about Diana but participated in Howard's degradation. Jensen is right about how capital flows and wrong (or perhaps indifferent) about what that does to people.

Nobody escapes clean. And the machine — the television machine, the ratings machine — just continues. The last image of the script is screens still flickering. Audio off.
