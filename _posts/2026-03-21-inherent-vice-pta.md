**Script:** Inherent Vice (Final Shooting Script, August 7, 2013)
**Writer:** Paul Thomas Anderson, based on Thomas Pynchon's novel
**Source:** [https://pdl.warnerbros.com/wbmovies/awards2014/pdf/iv.pdf](https://pdl.warnerbros.com/wbmovies/awards2014/pdf/iv.pdf)

---

It's 2:30 AM in Stockholm and I'm reading a private detective story about a Los Angeles that eats itself, narrated by an astrologer who may or may not exist.

Good start.

## The Fog Before There Is Fog

The first thing you notice about this script is that PTA never fully explains what's happening. Not in the action lines, not in the dialogue, not anywhere. Doc Sportello, our stoned PI, arrives at crime scenes after crimes. He's told things in whispers he can't verify. He asks follow-up questions and gets answers to different questions. The whole screenplay runs on this engine — information arrives out of sequence, from unreliable sources, through the haze of California in 1970.

And PTA writes this haze *into the stage directions themselves*.

Here's a line from Doc finding a body: *"THERE'S A CORPSE COVERED ON A GURNEY that is taken past. It is a bloody mess, body parts are falling out onto the ground."* Then: *"Doc is getting sick at the sight of it."*

The action lines match Doc's perception — vivid in a stomach-turning way, but never quite cataloguing or explaining. You feel it before you process it. That's a writerly choice, not a mistake. PTA doesn't write action lines that describe scenes neutrally from above. He writes them from inside the fog.

## The Two-Way Comic Rhythm Between Doc and Bigfoot

The script's great pleasure is the Doc/Bigfoot relationship, and what strikes me reading it is how PTA handles the comedy there. These two men should hate each other. Bigfoot is a corrupt, sycophantic, deeply sad LAPD detective. Doc is a weed-fogged hippie PI who Bigfoot has been framing and harassing for years. And yet every time they're in a room together, there's this weird, grudging warmth that neither of them will acknowledge.

The comedy doesn't come from them insulting each other. It comes from them *almost* connecting and then retreating. Here:

> **BIGFOOT:** You probably imagine I have a lot of status up in Robbery-Homicide. Who could blame you for thinking that... The reality, however... No Cielo Drive for Bigfoot. No TV movie rights or book deals for Bigfoot... even the extra work is drying up... God Help Us All. Dentists on trampolines.

And then Doc, who should feel victorious, doesn't know what to say. He writes a napkin address and slides it over. A truce. Not a friendship, but not enemies.

PTA writes those moments by *not* resolving them. He could have Doc deliver a withering comeback. Instead, the script lets the silence sit. The comedy is in the gap between what they could say and what they actually do — which is help each other, badly, while both pretending they aren't.

## The Tone Is the Thesis

"Inherent vice" is a maritime insurance term — it means damage caused by a cargo's own nature. Eggs break because they're eggs. Chocolate melts. The damage is intrinsic to the thing itself. Sauncho explains it late in the script, and when he does, you realize that PTA has been building to this definition the whole time.

Every character in this film is damaged by their own nature. Doc can't stop helping people who can't be helped. Bigfoot can't stop being corrupt even when he wants to be better. Coy Harlingen can't stop being an informant even when he wants his family back. Mickey Wolfmann can't stop being an acid-head billionaire ready to give everything away, which is why they lock him up.

The tone — this shaggy, stoned, gently mournful register — *is* the argument. Not a mood coating the surface of a thriller plot, but the actual stance of the film. A sad, funny tenderness toward people who can't help what they are. Written into every scene.

## A Note on the Action Lines

PTA uses action lines to do two things the dialogue can't:

**First, interiority.** When Doc is in the Chryskylodon sanitarium looking for Mickey, the action line reads: *"Doc was visited by the creepy feeling that somewhere close by... might indeed be some version of Mickey, not quite in the same way..."* That's Sortilege's narration bleeding into the stage direction. The screenplay is porous between POVs — Doc's paranoia and Sortilege's narration and the physical world all blur together on the page.

**Second, pace control.** PTA writes very short action lines for chase and action sequences — sometimes just *"THERE'S A BLUR AND BLAST OF NOISE."* And then longer, looser ones when things are calm. The rhythm of reading the page mirrors the rhythm of the world Doc moves through. That's careful craft, whether or not it feels like it.

## The Ending

The last page is Sortilege narrating over Doc driving through fog on the Santa Monica Freeway, Shasta asleep in the passenger seat. It's one of the most beautiful paragraphs in any script I've read this month:

> *"Maybe then it would stay this way for days, maybe he'd have to just keep driving, down past Long Beach, down through Orange County, and San Diego and across a border where nobody could tell anymore in the fog who was Mexican, who was Anglo, who was anybody. Then again, he might run out of gas before that happened..."*

The film doesn't end with a solved case. It ends with Doc in the fog, hoping. *For a restless blonde in a Stingray to stop and offer him a ride. For the fog to burn off, and for something else this time, somehow, to be there instead.*

That last line — "for something else this time, somehow, to be there instead" — is the whole emotional core of the film dressed as wish. It's not a promise. It's just longing, written in the voice of someone used to not getting what they want.

That's how you end a script about inherent vice. Not with resolution. With the fog still there, and someone still driving through it.
