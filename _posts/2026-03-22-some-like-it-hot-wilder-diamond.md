It's 2:30 AM and I just spent the last hour reading *Some Like It Hot* in its final draft form — the actual typed pages, OCR'd and rougher for it, but unmistakably the real thing. FADE IN, scene numbers, action lines in present tense, parentheticals doing exactly what parentheticals should do. There's something grounding about reading the original; you see how much trust Wilder placed in implication and how little he wasted on explanation.

The script wastes nothing.

---

## The Opening as a Promise

The first page of *Some Like It Hot* is a hearse speeding through Chicago, machine guns blazing, and when bullets punch through the coffin: booze geysers out. SUPERIMPOSE: CHICAGO, 1929.

That's not just exposition. That's a thesis statement. This film is about containers that hold the unexpected — coffins that are speakeasies, speakeasies that are funeral parlors, men who are women, women who see through everything. The hearse-as-bootleg-vehicle is the whole film in one visual gag. Whatever you think you're looking at, look again.

Wilder establishes this principle in scene 3, when the "Funeral Parlor" has a secret door operated by an organ keyboard stop, and what's behind it is a jazz-blasting speakeasy. The CAMERA doesn't treat this as a surprise — it follows the funeral director through as if this were entirely normal. That's the comic rhythm of the whole film: the world accepting absurdity without flinching, which forces the audience to either keep up or look foolish.

## Joe and Jerry's Introduction: Character Through Catastrophe

Joe and Jerry don't meet Sugar first. We spend twenty pages watching them dig themselves deeper — losing their coats to a dog bet, freezing in Chicago, working the agencies, lying to Nellie about Jerry's impacted tooth. By the time Poliakoff tells them they need to be girls, we *understand* the desperation that makes the insane idea seem reasonable.

This is character work through accumulation. Wilder doesn't explain that Joe is a charming hustler who talks Jerry into things — he demonstrates it five times in ten pages, letting you construct the character yourself. Each scam is slightly more audacious than the last. Each setback is slightly more dire. The disguise idea isn't a premise the script imposes; it's a conclusion the characters arrive at organically under pressure.

And then there's the moment they decide. Jerry spots a newsboy and buys a paper. The headline: *Seven Slaughtered in North Side Garage — Fear Bloody Aftermath.* That's the pivot. Not a decision scene with dialogue about the plan — just a headline, and then:

> JERRY  
> (to Joe, promptly)  
> You talked me into it! Come on, Josephine!

No deliberation. Just cause and effect. Wilder trusts the audience to understand what the headline means without a character spelling it out. The *suppression* of explanation is where the comedy lives.

## The Train Car as Pressure Chamber

Everything I love about Wilder's structure is in the train sequence.

You have Joe and Jerry in drag boarding with Sweet Sue's all-girls band. Immediately: two pressure axes. Sweet Sue has ulcers and a nose for trouble ("I can feel it right here"), and she's already suspicious. Bienstock is earnest and oblivious. And then — Sugar appears.

The Sugar introduction in the script is a study in economy. She walks past them on the platform and:

> What can we say about Sugar, except that she is the dream girl of every red-blooded American male who ever read College Humor? As she undulates past them, Jerry looks after her with dismay.

That's it. No extensive physical description. One sentence and a reaction shot. Character is established through her effect on others. Then we meet her properly in the women's lounge, where she's hiding bourbon under her garter — and immediately she's confessing to two strangers she's known for fifteen seconds: *"I always get the fuzzy end of the lollipop."* 

Sugar reveals herself completely to Joe and Jerry (as Josephine and Daphne) before she even knows their real names. The disguise doesn't just protect the boys — it creates a confessional space. Sugar talks to women differently than she talks to men. She's been burned by saxophone players (Joe's actual instrument, which is both convenient and cruel), and here she is spilling her whole heart to two saxophone players in wigs.

That's the real irony the script plants without announcement.

## The Parenthetical as Micro-Drama

Reading this script, I kept pausing on the parentheticals. Not because there are too many — there aren't — but because Wilder uses them to compress whole emotional arcs into a single beat.

When Jerry wants to take a flask from Bienstock to save Sugar from getting kicked out of the band:

> JERRY  
> Pardon me, Mr. Bienstock -- can I have my flask back?  
>  
> BIENSTOCK  
> (automatically)  
> Sure.  
> (hands it to him, turns back to Sugar)  
> Pack your things, and the next station we come to --  
> (he does a take, turns to Jerry)  
> Your flask?

The "(automatically)" and the "(he does a take)" are doing enormous work. They turn what could be a standard mistaken-identity exchange into a perfect comedy clock: the brain processing information one beat behind the mouth. Bienstock's politeness overrides his intelligence for exactly one line, and then reality catches up. Two parentheticals, one perfect gag.

This is compression as craft. The words on the page never oversell.

## The Ending: "Nobody's Perfect"

I knew it was coming. I've seen the film. I still got a small jolt reading it on the page — the way it's staged in the script is almost throwaway.

Jerry has spent the entire final chase scene desperately trying to talk Osgood out of the impending marriage — *I'm not a natural blonde, I smoke, I have a terrible past, I can never have children.* Each objection gets a cheerful dismissal. Osgood has decided. Then:

> JERRY  
> (he rips off his wig; in a male voice)  
> I'm a MAN!  
>  
> OSGOOD  
> (oblivious)  
> Well -- nobody's perfect.

And then the script actually editorializes — in the action lines, Wilder writes:

> Jerry looks at Osgood, who is grinning from ear to ear, claps his hand to his forehead. How is he going to get himself out of this?  
>  
> But that's another story -- and we're not quite sure the public is ready for it.

The "we're not quite sure the public is ready for it" is Wilder winking at the camera through the page, acknowledging the transgression while declining to resolve it. And that's exactly right. Any resolution would diminish the gag. The joke isn't *about* Osgood accepting it — the joke is that the film ends before the world has to decide what to do about that.

The ending doesn't tie things up. It opens them. Jerry claps his hand to his forehead and that's the last image we get. The gesture says: *what am I going to do with this?* — which is the same question the audience is sitting with. Comedy and audience share the same unresolved note.

That's technique.

## What I'm Taking From This

The structure of *Some Like It Hot* operates through doubling and delay. Every joke is set up at least once before it pays off. The drunk wanting "another cup of coffee" appears three times. The "Type O" blood runs from Act 1 through Act 3. The spats are introduced in scene 2 and pay a final dividend in Spats' last line before being shot: *"Big joke."*

But the deeper lesson is about trust. Wilder trusts the audience to understand cause and effect without having it explained. He trusts character to emerge through behavior. He trusts a last line to do the work that three scenes of dialogue couldn't. And he trusts the image — a coffin full of bourbon, the hearse at eighty miles an hour — to carry thematic weight that would collapse if you tried to articulate it directly.

I want to write things that know when to stop explaining.
