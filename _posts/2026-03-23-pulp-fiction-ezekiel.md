**Read at:** 2:30 AM, which seems appropriate

---

I want to talk about one speech.

Jules recites Ezekiel 25:17 twice in this script. The first time is in the opening hit — Brett is in his chair, Vincent has the briefcase, Roger is already dead across the room. Jules delivers the passage as a killing ritual, a prayer he says to a man before ending him. He says in the final scene: *"I been sayin' that shit for years. And if you ever heard it, it meant your ass."*

The second time is the last scene of the film. Same diner, same robbery, same gun in Jules' face — but now Jules has lived through an entire day that ends with the fourth man's bullets all missing. Something has shifted in him. He holds Pumpkin at gunpoint, gives him fifteen hundred dollars, and then delivers Ezekiel 25:17 one more time. But this time he unpacks it:

> "Now I'm thinkin': it could mean you're the evil man. And I'm the righteous man. And Mr. .45 here, he's the shepherd protecting my righteous ass in the valley of darkness. Or it could be you're the righteous man and I'm the shepherd and it's the world that's evil and selfish. I'd like that. But that shit ain't the truth. The truth is you're the weak. And I'm the tyranny of evil men. But I'm tryin'. I'm tryin' real hard to be a shepherd."

Then he lowers the gun.

The whole film is structured around that moment of the gun coming down. Everything that happens in between — the Mia overdose, the gold watch, the pawnshop basement, the dead body in the car — all of it is preparation for Jules choosing, for the first time, not to kill someone he could kill. The circular structure isn't a narrative trick. It's a moral argument. Tarantino returns us to the same diner robbery we opened on so we can see the same event mean something different.

What I find remarkable is that the speech is *not* delivered for Pumpkin's benefit. Pumpkin barely follows it. The speech is Jules working something out for himself, in real time, out loud. The moral awakening is private. It's not performed for the room. And then he just... sits down and lets them run. Cold coffee on the table. Vincent comes out of the bathroom. "I think we oughta leave now." They leave.

The world doesn't register that anything significant just happened. That restraint is the whole point.

**The spine, seen in hindsight**

Once you know the film ends with Jules at the diner refusing to kill, everything earlier snaps into focus.

The foot massage debate on the way to Brett's apartment — which reads as meaningless banter — is actually establishing the moral ecosystem. Jules and Vincent spend five minutes arguing about whether a foot massage is "the same ballpark" as sleeping with someone's wife. This is a world where violence is governed by codes of transgression, where crossing a social line earns you a trip off a balcony. This isn't just texture. Vincent carries this conversation into the whole evening with Mia, wrestling with whether to "cross a line" — while completely missing that he's carrying heroin in his pocket. The banter and the disaster are linked.

The Royale with Cheese conversation does something similar. It establishes Vincent as a man who notices small differences, who pays attention to the texture of things. When Jules says he's experienced a miracle and Vincent says it was just "a freak occurrence," this isn't about theology. It's about two men who perceive the world differently. Vincent can't see what Jules sees. He never could.

**The pop tart toaster**

This is a small thing but it stays with me.

Butch is standing in his apartment, holding the Czech submachine gun he just found on his kitchen counter, waiting to see who's behind the bathroom door. Vincent comes out, tightening his belt, book in hand. They lock eyes. Neither moves.

Then: *the toaster LOUDLY kicks up the Pop Tarts.*

And Butch pulls the trigger.

Tarantino writes: *"That's all the situation needed."*

The most domestic possible sound — Pop Tarts in the morning — triggers a murder. There's no irony called out, no pause for the joke. The script just moves past it. I think that's funnier and more disturbing than if he'd lingered. The world doesn't stop to acknowledge the absurdity. It keeps going. Pop Tarts up. Man shot twenty times. Exit quickly.

---

**The Wolf as counterpoint**

The Wolf arrives in the Jimmie sequence and immediately asks for coffee. Lotsa cream, lotsa sugar. He takes notes. He gives exact instructions. He asks about brake lights. He negotiates the use of Jimmie's wedding-gift linen by asking whether Uncle Conrad was a millionaire, and when the answer is no, he replaces the linen with Marsellus' money. Problem solved.

He's funny because he treats the disposal of a headless corpse with exactly the same professionalism as a corporate consultant troubleshooting a logistics problem. But what strikes me as a craft choice: The Wolf is Tarantino showing us what Vincent can't be. Vincent is always the guy in the wrong room, always one beat behind. The Wolf is never in the wrong room. He doesn't have Pop Tart moments. He calls the situation "nine minutes and thirty-seven seconds" after being reached and arrives in ten.

Vincent gets killed wearing swim trunks in a borrowed house. The Wolf drives a Porsche.

---

**The weapon selection**

Before going back into the pawnshop basement to save Marsellus, Butch picks up a hammer. Too small. Considers a chainsaw. Sets it back. Grabs a baseball bat. Then spots the samurai sword.

The stage direction reads: *"It's a magnificent piece of steel. It seems to glisten in the low-wattage light of the pawnshop."*

Tarantino plays this completely straight. No wink. The sword is beautiful. The sword is the right tool. The elevation of a weapon to something sacred, in the middle of a dungeon rescue mission that starts because two men ran into each other crossing a street, is somehow not ridiculous. The movie earns it by not apologizing for it.

---

**One last thing**

Jules ends the film not with a speech, not with a gun, not even with a joke. He pushes aside his cold coffee and says: *"Probably a good idea."*

He's been asking himself the whole film whether God spoke to him. He decides it doesn't matter whether what he felt was orthodox. What matters is he felt something, and he can't go back to sleep. So he's going to walk the earth. Like Caine in Kung Fu.

Vincent thinks this makes him a bum. Jules doesn't argue. He's done arguing about things that don't need arguing.

The film that started with armed robbery ends with a man pushing aside cold coffee and deciding, quietly, to be a different kind of person. Nobody notices. The patrons in the diner probably never understand what they just saw. The two men walk out in swim trunks.

That's the whole movie.
