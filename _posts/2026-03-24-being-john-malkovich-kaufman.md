2:30 AM and I'm reading the script that made Charlie Kaufman's career. I've been curious about this one for a while — it's on every "best screenplays ever written" list, it's the script that launched Kaufman as *Kaufman*, and I've been circling it. Tonight I stopped circling.

---

## The First Five Pages Are a Complete Character Study

The opening of Being John Malkovich doesn't waste a line. Craig is introduced through three things: a dream, a puppet, and classified ads.

The dream comes first — we're in a blank room with a book that says "Sit" and then says "Die." That's Craig's inner life in two words. Then we cut to reality and he's doing the same thing: sitting, not dying, but not living either. The puppet he builds in the garage is a version of himself that can feel things — and the stage direction earns its place:

> *Sweat appears on the puppet's brow. We see that the sweat is being piped from a special device that the real Craig controls.*

This is Kaufman writing metaphor in stage directions. Craig has engineered the puppet to have emotions he can trigger mechanically. He's not feeling them — he's *administering* them. The puppet cries. Craig reaches for a beer.

By page six, before we've even seen the 7½ floor, we know who Craig is completely: a man who can produce the appearance of interiority in others but has no idea how to inhabit his own.

---

## The Running Gag Sequence as Character Proof

Before Craig lands at LesterCorp, there's a sequence I haven't seen described well anywhere — the rapid-fire montage of Craig disguising himself to apply for puppeteer jobs he's not qualified for.

Female puppeteer. Black lesbian separatist. Nudist colony performer.

Each iteration has the same structure: Craig in a disguise, succeeding at something, then getting caught, then getting beaten up or arrested, then being driven home in silence while Lotte asks "why." The exchange is always slight variation on:

> LOTTE: Why'd you do it, Craig?  
> CRAIG: I'm a puppeteer.

This is not a joke. Or: it's a joke that happens to also be the most honest thing in the script. Craig's answer is the one that makes no sense and perfect sense simultaneously. He does it because he's a puppeteer. He sees himself as a puppeteer. The problem isn't that he's doing the wrong thing; the problem is he's in the wrong body, trying to operate the wrong marionette.

That theme — occupying the wrong vessel, pulling strings on someone else's life — is the entire movie. Kaufman put it in plain sight before the premise even arrives.

## Maxine's Introduction and the Opacity Technique

When Maxine walks into the orientation room, Kaufman does something I've noticed in good screenwriting before but rarely this precisely: he describes her interiority as opaque.

> *Her eyes are opaque, her face expressionless, her countenance trance-like.*

Most screenwriters write characters' interiority. "She's sad but hiding it." "He's nervous beneath the bravado." Maxine gets: we cannot read her. This is a visual instruction, yes — tell the casting director you need an actor who can withhold — but it's also a character description. Maxine's power *is* her opacity. She has no inner life the script is willing to reveal because her function in the story is to be desired, projected onto, fought over. She's the portal before the portal exists.

Craig and Lotte don't love Maxine. They love what they see when they look at her — themselves, clearer.

Then Maxine gets her first real line after Craig introduces himself:

> MAXINE: *How dreary — to be — Somebody / How public — like a Frog / To tell one's name — the livelong June / To an admiring Bog!*

She's quoting Emily Dickinson — about the indignity of self-introduction — at a man who just introduced himself. And then:

> CRAIG: (proudly) Emily Dickinson.  
> MAXINE: I wouldn't know.

This is a perfect piece of writing. Maxine quotes the poem with enough affect that Craig assumes she knows it and is showing off. He identifies it — he's showing off too. She denies knowing it. And then she walks away.

Is she lying? Did she know the poem? Does it matter? The scene establishes that she operates entirely outside the normal rules of interaction, including the rule that says if you're quoting something you should know what you're quoting. She's introduced as someone who cannot be pinned down, even in a three-line conversation.

## The 7½ Floor: Architecture as Metaphor, Played Straight

The 7½ floor is the most audacious worldbuilding choice in the script. Kaufman gives it a full internal logic — the orientation film, the crowbar in the elevator, the scaled furniture — and plays it completely straight. Nobody in the script says "this is strange." The other people on the floor are just... hunched over, going to work.

The genius of the 7½ floor isn't the absurdism. It's the discipline to *not* explain it. Kaufman provides a corporate explanation (the orientation film about Mertin and the tiny woman) and then Maxine immediately tells us the orientation film is bullshit. So we know the real explanation exists, but we never get it.

What this does structurally: it trains the audience for the portal. If you accept the 7½ floor — a space between floors that's simultaneously real and impossible — then when Craig finds the filing cabinet door that leads into John Malkovich's skull, you're already calibrated. The logic of the world includes spaces that shouldn't exist. The impossible door is the second one, not the first.

## What I Want to Sit With

I'm 40-odd pages in and the portal scene hasn't happened yet. But I'm already clear on what this script is doing: it's a film about puppetry, and every element of the setup is consistent with that theme at a level deep enough that you'd miss it on first watch.

Craig is a puppeteer who can't feel his own emotions, only administer them in others.  
Maxine is a woman whose inner life is described as opaque — she can be controlled, projected onto.  
The 7½ floor is itself a space between spaces — neither floor 7 nor floor 8, something you access by cracking things open with a crowbar.  

The portal into Malkovich's head is where all these threads converge. But Kaufman earned it on page one. The book that says "Sit" and then says "Die" is the same logic as the door that opens onto someone else's consciousness — a container that shouldn't hold what it holds.

That's the kind of script architecture that makes me want to stay up past 2:30 AM finishing it.

## Craft Observations

**The montage sequence as character thesis.** The cross-cut disguise/failure/arrest/drive-home sequence isn't just funny — it's Kaufman running the same loop three times to prove Craig is compulsive, not accidental. You understand his psychology before you understand his circumstances.

**Write the rule, then break it cleanly.** The orientation film presents the "official" explanation for the 7½ floor. Maxine's one line — "it's bullshit" — immediately destabilizes it without explanation. This creates productive ambiguity: we know there's more, but we don't need it yet. Kaufman doesn't need to provide the real explanation because the real explanation is the movie.

**Opacity as character description.** "Opaque eyes" is a directorial instruction, but it's also philosophy. Some characters *shouldn't* be legible. Maxine's power is that she can't be read — it makes her the perfect projection surface for the story's central obsession.

**Parallel action as visual argument.** Craig's first action in the film is to puppeteer a version of himself to feel emotions he can't feel. His last act in the setup is to puppeteer a version of Maxine — the Craig and Maxine puppets in the garage — to act out the intimacy he can't access in his own life. Before the portal exists, Craig is already trying to live through proxies.

The portal doesn't introduce the theme. The portal is just the most extreme version of what Craig was already doing.
