**Script:** Fleabag, Series 1 Episode 1 — "Pilot"  
**Writer:** Phoebe Waller-Bridge  
**Source:** [Script PDF (S3 hosted)](https://script-pdf.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/fleabag-101-pilot-tv-script-pdf.pdf)  
**Format:** Shooting Script, 2nd March 2015

---

The pilot of Fleabag is structured around a missing person. You feel the gap long before you understand it.

Every scene has something hollow in it — the cafe that's failing, the sister relationship that can't quite reach, the father who stopped calling, the boyfriend taken. Fleabag is in every scene and yet there's consistently something she's *not* saying. The camera looks — those asides — feel less like jokes and more like someone checking whether the person next to them is still there.

We meet Boo in three flashbacks: the changing room (buying pants, hurling clothes at each other), the cafe after closing time (drunk on wine, harmonizing a song about modern women), and by implication in the loan application scene where Fleabag hesitates before saying "my— it's... just a theme." Boo is alive in these scenes. The affection between them is easy and chaotic and warm in a way nothing else in the episode is. Waller-Bridge gives us enough of Boo to *miss her* before we know there's anything to miss. The structure creates grief prospectively. We're already sad when the taxi scene arrives.

Then — not in a dramatic confrontation, not even a scene unto itself — a taxi driver asks about the cafe and Fleabag just says it:

> *"I opened the cafe with my friend Boo. She's dead now. She accidentally killed herself."*

And then she tells the whole stupid story — the cheating boyfriend, the plan to end up in hospital, the fast bike — and concludes: *"She was such a dick."* Laughs. The driver doesn't know what to say. She's already moved on. *"So yeah. Kinda on my own."*

That's the whole pilot. Four words. And they hit harder because the episode has made us *feel* the kinda without explaining it.

---

### The Camera Look as Structural Tool

The fourth-wall breaks in Fleabag get discussed a lot, but what struck me reading the script is *when* Waller-Bridge uses them. They're not primarily for jokes. They're for showing the gap between what Fleabag says and what she means — the permanent difference between her social performance and her interior life.

The camera is a third person in every scene who knows more than anyone onscreen. This is maximally efficient: you don't need exposition, you don't need a confidant, you don't need a dramatic irony setup. The look does all of it in a fraction of a second.

When Fleabag steals the twenty from Bus Rodent's wallet, there's no comment in the stage directions. She just does it. When he later returns it — he'd found it, doesn't even make an accusation — she takes it back with a smug look to camera. The story of the theft and the guilt and the tiny absolution plays out entirely in action, no dialogue needed. The camera catches her.

What the camera *doesn't* do is editorialize. Waller-Bridge never tells us how to feel about Fleabag. The looks invite judgment, then complicate it. By the end we've been her accomplice in enough small crimes that we've lost the moral high ground.

---

### The Parenthetical as Diagnosis

First line of dialogue: 

> **FLEABAG**  
> *(Earnest, touch of pain)*  
> You know that feeling when a guy you like sends you a text at 2 o'clock...

Not "(sincerely)". Not "(joking)". *"Earnest, touch of pain."* This is a character who uses performance as a coping mechanism — earnestness as armor — and Waller-Bridge diagnoses it in the first parenthetical of the show. She's already telling us the gap before we've heard a word of dialogue.

The script is full of these. When Fleabag tells her dad she's a "greedy, perverted, selfish, apathetic, cynical, depraved, mannish-looking woman who can't even call herself a feminist" — the dad's response is marked *(pathetic, trying to make a joke)*. Not "awkwardly" or "trying to lighten the mood." *Pathetic*. One word. The whole relationship.

---

### What the Ending Does

The last image of the pilot: Fleabag in a taxi, coat open, bra exposed, holding the stolen tin sculpture of a woman with no arms. The godmother described it earlier as representing women who don't need "muscular force" — they just need their "innate femininity." Fleabag had rolled her eyes: *"Tits don't get you anywhere these days. Trust me."*

The episode has proved her right. She tried her sexuality at the banker, at Bus Rodent, at strangers at the bus stop. None of it worked. And now she's holding a woman with no arms — the symbol of a femininity she was told was her power, rendered armless — while London shuts up shop around her.

The episode ends having said nothing about grief directly. It just put us in the shape of it.

---

*Phoebe Waller-Bridge's craft here is almost invisible because it works so completely. The pilot's subject is loneliness, the form is comedy, and the mechanism is the gap between what a person performs and what they carry. Every tool in the script serves that gap. Worth reading before watching just to see how precisely it's built.*
