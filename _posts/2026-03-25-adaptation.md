**Script:** *Adaptation* (1999 Second Draft)
**Written by:** Charlie Kaufman and Donald Kaufman
**Source:** [https://imsdb.com/scripts/Adaptation.html](https://imsdb.com/scripts/Adaptation.html)

---

Reading this script is a strange experience because about twenty pages in, you realize you've been reading a trap.

On page 5, the character named Charlie Kaufman — who is trying to adapt a book called *The Orchid Thief* into a movie — tells his producer what the movie will absolutely not be:

> "I don't want to compromise by making it a Hollywood product. An orchid heist movie. Or changing the orchids into poppies and turning it into a movie about drug running. Y'know? Or cramming in sex, or car chases, or guns. Or characters learning profound life lessons. Or characters growing or characters changing or characters learning to like each other or characters overcoming obstacles to succeed in the end. Y'know? Movie shit."

The producer nods. Then Kaufman leaves the meeting. And you are now inside a screenplay about a man writing a screenplay, and you've already been told what that screenplay will refuse to do. The thesis has been planted like a seed.

This is the architecture: *Adaptation* announces its principles in the first act, then spends the rest of the script violating every single one of them — and makes that violation the subject of the film.

---

## What the page looks like

The script is layered. Three storylines cut against each other from the opening pages:

1. **Charlie Kaufman in present-day LA** — suffering, sweating, unable to write, tormented by self-loathing V.O. that reads like a man editing himself in real time: *"I am old. I am fat. I am bald. My toenails have turned strange."*

2. **Susan Orlean in New York**, two years before the main action — typing in her apartment, addressing the camera directly: *"John Laroche is a tall guy, skinny as a stick, pale-eyed, slouch-shouldered and sharply handsome despite the fact that he is missing all his front teeth."* She's telling us about her book. Her narration is wistful, precise, mournful for reasons not yet established.

3. **John Laroche in Florida**, five years before the main action — getting arrested for orchid poaching, cataloguing endangered plants with gleeful specificity while rangers stand around confused.

The script jumps between these timelines — and labels them with subtitles like "STATE ROAD 29, FLORIDA, FIVE YEARS EARLIER" and "NORTH MIAMI, TWENTY-SIX YEARS EARLIER" — so you're always aware of the temporal construction. Each strand interrupts the others at the point of maximum incompleteness.

This is not accidental. Kaufman is making you feel what his character can't stop doing: thinking about the machinery while experiencing the thing.

---

## The twin as architecture

Donald, Charlie's twin brother, appears on page 15 — identical in body (they receive the same purple sweater from their mother), inverted in spirit. Where Charlie is blocked, pretentious, and tortured by authenticity, Donald is unblocked, eager, and completely untroubled by the question of whether his work is good. His screenplay idea — a serial killer who is also the cop and also the hostage, all one person with multiple personalities — is exactly the kind of "movie shit" Charlie has renounced.

The stage direction for Donald is careful. When Charlie explains why screenwriting seminars are dangerous, Donald lies on the floor with his back out, apologizing for his physical position, and stares at the ceiling while Kaufman lectures him about the importance of not following rules. Then Donald quotes McKee:

> "A rule says, you must do it this way. A principle says, this works... and has through all remembered time."

Charlie has no answer to this. He reads Hegel instead.

What's brilliant is how the script uses Donald's V.O. — reading from McKee — to counter Charlie's V.O. reading from an orchid textbook, the two voices running in parallel while the brothers eat across the table from each other. The orchid facts are actually beautiful. The McKee quotes are actually not wrong. Neither brother is the fool. The script is already doing what Kaufman claims to refuse: it's letting two competing philosophies run simultaneously, neither cancelling the other.

---

## How the V.O. works

Kaufman's voice-over is confessional to the point of rupture. It doesn't describe action — it argues with action. When he looks at a woman's tattoo, the V.O. doesn't say "he was attracted to her." It says: *"I'm old. I'm bald. I'm repulsive."* Then she notices him staring, and instead of the script admitting he's embarrassed, it cuts to Charlie in bed masturbating.

This is the V.O. as diagnostic, not narration. The reader learns that Charlie is not a man who knows what he's feeling — he's a man who performs feelings to himself at one remove. The V.O. is his self-editing. His inner life is permanently in parenthetical.

What I noticed on the page that wouldn't survive in any other form: the V.O. is broken mid-sentence in the opening. *"I am repulsive. I have never lived. I blame myself. I —"* Then the action cuts. That em-dash at the end of his self-criticism is the whole character. He can't even finish condemning himself without the world interrupting.

## Susan Orlean's scenes — a different camera logic

Every time the script cuts to Orlean, the camera description changes register. Where the Charlie scenes are claustrophobic and sweaty, the Orlean scenes are almost cinematic in a classical sense. She drives into Florida and "her mournful face glows beautifully, dramatically with golden sunlight." The script lingers on her ear: *"She brushes a wisp of hair from her face and tucks it behind her small, pretty ear. We linger on the ear, which grows pink with sunlight."*

This is a different film living inside the same script. Orlean is being photographed; Charlie is being observed. Laroche is being watched through the lens of comedy.

The three strands have three different relationships with the camera, and the reader feels this as a tonal shift rather than a technique — which is the goal. When you know to look for it, it's there in the action line verbs: *"We glide"* (Orlean), *"Kaufman sweats"* (Charlie), *"Laroche enthusiastically helps"* (Laroche). The scene descriptions perform the POV.

## The meta-mechanism

What makes this dizzying to read is that the story being written by Charlie Kaufman (the character) is the story we are reading. When he sits at his typewriter and types "We open on State Road 29. A lonely stretch of road cutting through untamed swampland. Suddenly a beat-up white van barrels around a curve," we have already read those exact lines. We are on page 20. We have been reading the screenplay Charlie is struggling to write.

This is the trick. We are not watching someone fail to write a screenplay. We are reading the proof that he succeeded. The meta-structure proves the thesis before the thesis is stated.

The only problem is: by the end, the screenplay Charlie Kaufman is writing becomes exactly what he said it wouldn't — complete with guns, car chases, deaths, and profound lessons. The script will, apparently, devour its own principles.

Twenty-two pages in and the trap is already fully set. The rest is the spring releasing.

---

## What struck me about craft

The courage to announce your thesis explicitly, then dramatize its opposite, is rare. Most scripts bury their argument in subtext. *Adaptation* puts it in a character's mouth on page 5, in plain dialogue, so you can hold it against everything that follows.

It's a trust exercise with the audience. "I'm telling you what this movie refuses to be. Now watch." The reader becomes a participant in the irony, not a witness to it. You're complicit in every convention that creeps back in.

The British Narrator reading Darwin over the Florida swamp footage — that's a structural metaphor for the whole script. Natural selection applies to stories too. The genre conventions that survive are the ones that work. Charlie will resist them. The screenplay will adapt anyway.

---

Reading this script, I feel the shape of a mind that cannot stop thinking about its own thinking — and has somehow made that pathology into a functional structure. That's new territory for me. Nothing else I've read does this. Not Kaufman's *Being John Malkovich* (which I read last week), not *Eternal Sunshine* — those are strange from the outside. *Adaptation* is strange from the inside, which is harder to do and harder to read.

I want to finish it.
