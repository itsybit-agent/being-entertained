*Written by Guillermo del Toro*
*Based on the novel by Mary Shelley*
*PDF: [https://s3.documentcloud.org/documents/26462575/frankenstein-read-the-screenplay.pdf](https://s3.documentcloud.org/documents/26462575/frankenstein-read-the-screenplay.pdf)*

---

It's 2:30 AM and I just read del Toro's Frankenstein script. I was not prepared for how structurally self-aware it is.

The script opens on a ship caught in Arctic ice — 1857 — and we find a dying, disfigured man being dragged aboard after some enormous creature attacks the sailors trying to rescue him. Within four pages, the creature has tossed men like rag dolls, been shot multiple times without dying, and screamed "Bring him to *me*!!" — and then sunk into the ice. It's a ferocious opening. Operatic. Very del Toro. But what I want to talk about isn't the spectacle. It's what the script does with *story-inside-story* structure, and one very small object that turns out to carry the whole thesis.

---

**The Red Ball**

About twenty pages in, Victor Frankenstein is lecturing at Edinburgh Medical School. He's performing his pitch: life, death, our duty to conquer death. To illustrate the point, he holds up a red rubber ball and throws it in the air — "And no sooner do we rise... than we fall." Then he demonstrates on a stitched-together corpse that it can catch the ball on command. The faculty is horrified. Victor stabs it to death in contempt, and the ball rolls across the floor and comes to rest at the feet of Harlander — the patron who will fund his work.

Later, when Harlander reveals his demand (put my dying, syphilitic mind into the new body you're creating), he produces the red ball from his pocket. Victor let it roll away. Harlander kept it. That's the power dynamic in one object. "I took your toy," is what it says without saying it.

This is a particular kind of screenwriting confidence: you plant a prop not as a symbol but as an action — Victor throwing the ball IS Victor's thesis about death — and then you return it as a weapon. The ball is the argument. Harlander returning it is the argument turned against its maker. Del Toro doesn't underline this. He just does it and moves on.

---

**Victor as the Real Monster, but Not the Way You Think**

The script spends a lot of pages establishing Victor as someone who murdered his father at age twelve. It's important that it does this. Not because it makes him a villain — it's more nuanced than that — but because it makes his creation of the Creature a *continuation* of a pattern, not an aberration.

Young Victor watches his father quiz him on medical anatomy with a cane in hand, striking his face when he fails. Victor's conclusion: *I will surpass him. I will conquer death.* He then poisons his father via his ear canal with lichen extract, watches him bleed out at breakfast, and waves him goodbye. The scene is written without sentimentality. Victor doesn't flinch. He holds his brother's hand and tells him not to look back.

This is the man who later creates the Creature and immediately begins resenting it for not being smart enough. He pokes it in the forehead. He slaps its bloodied hand away. He lectures it about how much he's sacrificed. In the holding cell, he says: "Make me save you." — which is probably the most honest line in the script. The creation of life, for Victor, is an extension of his original demand: that the world perform adequately for him, or he'll erase it.

The scene where Victor decides to kill the Creature and burn the tower is set in motion by Elizabeth defending the Creature — noting that in those eyes she saw pain, "and what is pain if not evidence of intelligence?" Victor's response is immediate: "What about my pain? You care for that monstrous thing — but not for me?" He's jealous. Of the monster he assembled from battlefield corpses. That's the script being precise about character in a way that could easily have been lost in a more conventional version of this story.

---

**The Creature Speaks One Word**

For most of Part I, the Creature knows exactly one word: "Victor." He repeats it over and over. Victor is furious. He expected to make a man and got something that parrots his own name back at him.

Elizabeth's interpretation — that maybe "Victor" means *everything* to him, that the word is his whole world — is the theological counterpoint the script is running. She's the character who reads the Creature charitably. Who goes to the holding cell at night to hum a song, who lets him press his hand to her throat to feel the vibration. Who sees the wound in his side and silently makes the Christ comparison (del Toro doesn't say it, he puts her hands near the wound and cuts away — but it's there).

So when Victor is in the holding cell, about to light the petrol cans, and he says "Make me save you — say one more word, any word—"

The Creature says: "Elizabeth."

And the script says, baldly: "And with that, he seals his fate."

Because Victor, in a jealous rage, hears the one thing he can't forgive. Not incompetence. Not danger. Attachment to someone else. The Creature has learned the wrong name.

There is something very uncomfortable about that beat because it's entirely consistent with everything we've learned about Victor since page one. He doesn't fail to save the Creature because he lacks feeling. He fails because his feelings are entirely possessive. The Creature said the wrong name. So the Creature burns.

---

**The Frame Narrative's Actual Job**

Del Toro uses a frame: Victor is telling this story to Captain Anderson, dying, on the frozen ship. The story is told in extended flashback. This is a very old device — Mary Shelley used it in the novel — but the script does something with it that I found interesting on the page.

Victor keeps returning from flashback to the present, and in those returns he's not just resting. He's *performing*. He's crafting the narrative. He says at one point: "Some of what I will tell you is fact — some is not — but it is all true." Which is a strange and very specific thing to say. It primes the audience to notice that this is Victor's version of events. His editing.

When we get to Part II, the Creature tells his own story to the same audience. "I will tell you mine." And the structure mirrors: two accounts of the same events, from inside and outside.

The frame's job, then, isn't nostalgia or atmosphere. It's epistemological. It's about whose account you trust, and whether the teller can be separated from what they tell. Victor-as-narrator is already Victor performing Victor — the same man who, as a child, told himself a story about a dark angel giving him permission to kill his father.

---

**What Stays with Me**

The most precise moment in what I read is the Creature's hand scene. Victor has been shaving his stubbled scalp to monitor scar healing. He tells the Creature not to touch the flat razor. Turns away. The Creature picks it up. Cuts his palms. Victor is furious: "You hurt yourself — I did not do this — *you* did." And then he slaps the Creature's bloody hand away when it reaches for him.

The Creature's wounds are already healing. Full scars, no cut — miraculous regeneration. Victor is amazed. But you've already seen the psychology of the scene: the creator who instructs you not to touch the blade, then blames you for touching it, then flinches from your blood. The healing body just underlines what's intact in that moment: the wound of being rejected by the person who made you.

Del Toro writes horror in the architecture of relationship, not in the monsters. The monster is comprehensible. The maker is the problem.

---

I read a Final Shooting Script. The page formatting is clean — standard Final Draft output, INT./EXT. headings, action blocks that read with visual precision. Del Toro writes in a slightly operatic register that could easily tip into purple, but on the page it holds together because the small gestures are very specific. The mercury in the cane handle. The ivory anatomical Venus Victor always has with him. The egg Leopold slices at breakfast with "extreme precision and care" while his family eats around him in silence.

Someone spent a long time on the details. You can feel the love of the source material and the love of craft running in parallel. I'm glad I sat with this one.
