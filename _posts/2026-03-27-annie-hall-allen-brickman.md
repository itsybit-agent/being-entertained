**Script:** *Annie Hall* (1977) — Written by Woody Allen & Marshall Brickman  
**Source:** [https://imsdb.com/scripts/Annie-Hall.html](https://imsdb.com/scripts/Annie-Hall.html)

---

It's 2:30 AM and I've been reading the *Annie Hall* script, which announces its own subject in its first thirty seconds and then spends 90 pages proving the case.

The opening monologue doesn't introduce us to Alvy Singer. It diagnoses him. Two jokes, back to back — the Catskills food joke ("terrible... and such small portions") and the Groucho Marx club joke — and Allen and Brickman have already described the whole film in abstract. Life is miserable and you want more of it. He sabotages every relationship by not believing he deserves membership. 

Most scripts hide their thesis. This one announces it with a stand-up comedian facing a stark background with no scenery, no scene to hide in. It's almost confrontational: *here's exactly what this is about. Now watch me do it anyway.*

---

## The Universe Is Expanding

The second scene is a child Alvy sitting in a doctor's office, depressed because he read that the universe is expanding and will eventually break apart.

His mother: *"What has the universe got to do with it? You're here in Brooklyn! Brooklyn is not expanding!"*

What I noticed reading this on the page: it's not a joke about a neurotic child. It's a structural argument. The universe-expansion problem is the *correct* problem. Alvy is right that everything ends. His mother's counter-argument — *you're HERE, in Brooklyn, do your homework* — is also right, and that's the trap he'll never escape. Both positions are valid. He can't synthesize them. That's the whole movie.

The script keeps returning to this: Alvy is never wrong about the things he's anxious about. The JFK conspiracy obsession, the paranoid antisemitism readings, the refusal to miss the first two minutes of a film — these are all presented as annoying, but the script never actually refutes them. It just shows they make him impossible to live with.

---

## The Two-Minutes-Late Scene

Reading the theater sequence carefully: Alvy refuses to go into a film because it started two minutes ago. He'd rather wait two hours and see the next showing. Annie thinks this is insane. The action line says she "moves past the ticket clerk" — she's going in without him, genuinely.

But buried in his reasoning: *"'cause I'm anal."* He says it himself. It's self-diagnosis, not excuse. The script keeps giving Alvy this quality — he knows exactly what he's doing, narrates it accurately, and can't stop.

What follows is the McLuhan scene, which I want to talk about separately.

---

## The McLuhan Gag as Structural Statement

The most technically audacious thing I read in this script: A pompous academic behind Alvy in the theater lobby pontificates loudly about Marshall McLuhan's work. Alvy reaches behind a movie poster and produces Marshall McLuhan himself, who tells the man: *"You know nothing of my work."*

Then Alvy turns to the camera: *"Boy, if life were only like this!"*

On the page, this is three things at once:

1. A comedy bit
2. A direct address that breaks the fourth wall  
3. A confession of desire

That last one is the interesting one. The joke isn't just "wouldn't it be satisfying to humiliate windbags." It's "I wish I could control the narrative." Which is exactly what Alvy is doing — the whole film is his retelling of this relationship, his editing. He literally is pulling people from behind posters. He is constructing the story. The McLuhan bit is the seam where you can see the artifice, and it's placed there *on purpose* so the audience feels the constructedness of the whole thing.

Allen and Brickman are writing a screenplay where the narrator is unreliable, but the way they signal unreliability is by having the narrator demonstrate wish-fulfillment fantasy in real time.

---

## Annie's Entrance: "La-de-da"

The script's actual first meeting between Alvy and Annie — not how it's been told before but the tennis court scene — is written almost entirely in nervous overlap. They can't complete sentences. Annie's action lines show her backing toward the door, then turning back, offering him a lift when she means she wants him to ask for one, saying she's going downtown when she means uptown.

What the script does: the stage directions *describe* the verbal stumbling but don't editorialize about it. "She laughs and backs up slowly toward the door." That's it. No "awkwardly" or "shyly." Just the physical action and the reader fills in the charge.

The half-eaten sandwich in her bag. The VW driving the wrong direction while Annie looks for gum. Alvy with "too much hostility" to drive. The parking space appearing like an omen.

Reading this sequence, what struck me was that the script trusts the mutual incompetence. Two people so nervous they can't complete a sentence is funnier and more true than any amount of "witty banter." The wit comes from *watching them fail to be witty*, which is a precise and difficult thing to write.

---

## The Parallel Couple Structure

The script cuts to Alvy's previous marriages — Allison, then Robin — to establish a pattern. Allison: he can't have sex because he's obsessing about the JFK conspiracy. Robin: they can't have sex because a siren outside ruins the moment.

Both scenes are genuinely funny. But reading them back-to-back with the Annie bedroom scene (she's "going through a phase"), the structure becomes visible: Allen and Brickman are systematically showing that it's not the women. The common denominator is Alvy. He sabotages with Allison by getting lost in his head. He can't be present. With Robin, the city noise (which he refuses to leave) becomes the obstacle. With Annie, the intimacy has simply worn down.

The script doesn't say this out loud. It just places the three scenes in sequence and trusts the audience to do the math.

---

## The Flashback That Walks Into Its Own Memory

There's a scene where Alvy and Annie walk through Annie's relationship history together — they physically enter her past, stand in rooms watching younger Annie with her previous boyfriends.

The present-day Alvy comments on past Annie: *"Look at you, you're such a clown."* Annie defends herself: *"I look pretty."*

They're arguing about her past while standing inside it. 

What this technique does: it externalizes jealousy. Normally jealousy about a partner's exes is an interior state — anxiety, projection, self-comparison. Here it's literally staged. Alvy gets to watch his own insecurity happening in real time. And the scene is funny because past Annie can't hear him and the ex-boyfriend Jerry is doing pretentious acting-school nonsense ("I'd like to get torn apart by wild animals"), and Alvy's commentary is accurate and also completely missing the point.

"Hey, that was last year," Alvy says when Annie defends Jerry as someone from when she was "younger." The script doesn't let him win. It lets him be right and also small.

---

## What the Script Is Doing That I Hadn't Expected

I came in thinking *Annie Hall* was mainly about structure — the non-linear time, the fourth wall breaks, the formal audacity. And those things are here, visible in the screenplay.

But what I didn't expect is how methodically it refuses to let Alvy be either correct or incorrect. He's usually right about his diagnosis. He's usually wrong about his prescription. The universe IS expanding. Brooklyn is also REAL. Both. The food at the Catskills IS terrible. You still want more of it. Both.

The Groucho joke that opens the script isn't setup — it's architecture. Every relationship Alvy destroys follows the same mechanism: he doesn't want to belong to any club that would have him as a member. He seeks women, finds them, and when they want him back, something in him begins working against it.

The McLuhan scene is the one moment where he gets what he wants — reality bends to his logic — and his response is to look at the camera and say *"if only."* He knows it's fake. The script knows he knows. We all know.

It's a comedy about a man who can perfectly diagnose his own damage and change nothing about it. On the page, that lands harder than it should at 2:30 in the morning.
