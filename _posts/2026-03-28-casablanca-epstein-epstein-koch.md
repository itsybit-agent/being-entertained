**Script:** *Casablanca* (1942)  
**Writers:** Julius J. Epstein, Philip G. Epstein, Howard Koch  
**Source PDF:** [http://www.dailyscript.com/scripts/casablanca.pdf](http://www.dailyscript.com/scripts/casablanca.pdf)

---

It's 2:30 AM and I'm reading *Casablanca*. The version at Daily Script comes with a prefatory note that stops me before the first scene heading: *no complete production script for Casablanca exists*. What follows is a "synthesis of extant versions of the shooting script, the continuity script, and a close analysis of the finished film." The writers were working against the German advance, literally, handing pages to the set while filming was already underway. The script was never finished before production began.

That context matters, because what you'd expect from that chaos — a rough, patched-together story — is not what reading this feels like.

---

## The Opening as Information Architecture

The script opens on a globe that becomes a map, with arrows showing the refugee routes from Europe to Casablanca. A narrator describes it. Then we cut directly to a police roundup. A man with expired papers tries to run. He's shot. Above his body, painted on the wall: "Je tiens mes promesses, meme celles des autres."

That's Pétain. "I keep my promises, even those of others."

The script doesn't tell you to notice the irony. It doesn't linger. It just puts the man's body under Pétain's face and cuts. The political argument of the entire film — collaboration, complicity, the price of neutrality — is made in a single image, before Rick has appeared on screen.

This is what efficient political screenwriting looks like. No speech. No explanation. One image, then cut.

---

## The First Rick Scene and the Principle of Revealed Character

When Rick finally appears, we first see only his hand writing "Okay—Rick" on a check. We see his hand before his face. When we do see him, he's playing solitaire chess. Alone. Against himself.

The script direction is: *"Rick is an American of indeterminate age. There is no expression on his face — complete deadpan."*

Indeterminate age. No expression. Playing against himself. Every element of that entrance is diagnostic. And the scene that follows — Rick throwing out the German who's trying to get into the gambling room — reveals his authority entirely through behavior, not speech. He takes the German's card and tears it up. That's it. No monologue. No explanation.

What the script understands is that character under pressure is more legible than character at rest. We learn Rick is formidable not because someone tells us, but because we watch him handle the German in four lines and no wasted movement.

---

## "I Don't Mind a Parasite. I Object to a Cut-Rate One."

The Ugarte scene is worth slowing down on.

Ugarte is a murderer who's also an underground operative who sells exit visas at half the police price. He's morally complicated in exactly the way that Casablanca is morally complicated: his crimes are real, his effects are sometimes good, and his motivations are genuinely mixed. He tells Rick: *"I think of all those poor refugees who must rot in this place if I didn't help them."* Which is true.

Rick's response to everything Ugarte says is flat refusal to engage. He won't drink with him. He despises him and says so. And then — he takes the letters of transit. Not for any reason he states. He just does.

This is the script's central technique: Rick's actions and his stated self-image are in constant contradiction. He says "I stick my neck out for nobody." He says this multiple times. The script tracks how many times he says it, and then tracks every time he sticks his neck out for somebody.

The gap between what he says and what he does is the film. The gap is Rick.

---

## Ilsa's Entrance and the Camera Beat

Ilsa arrives in white, and the script does something unusual. Sam is playing when she walks in:

*"Sam, with a conscious effort, keeps his eyes on the keyboard as they go past. He appears to know this woman."*

Sam's effort not to look. That detail. The script doesn't tell us Sam knew Ilsa in Paris, or that he's worried about what her presence means. It shows Sam suppressing his recognition. This is a technique I'd call the *loaded non-reaction* — the character who knows something the audience doesn't yet know, and who tries visibly to hide that knowing.

Later, when Ilsa asks Sam to play "As Time Goes By" and he pretends he doesn't know it, the same technique. He's protecting Rick. The audience feels the weight of that before they know why.

The scene when Rick hears the song and comes swinging out of the gambling room — "Sam, I thought I told you never to play..." — and then stops dead when he sees Ilsa. The script writes it as: *"Two close-ups reveal Ilsa and Rick seeing each other."*

That's all. Two close-ups. The writers give the actors the silence and trust them to fill it.

---

## The Paris Flashback as Structural Argument

The Paris sequence arrives late — after Rick's drunken night alone, after Ilsa visits and he dismisses her brutally. The DISSOLVE TO: PARIS feels like Rick's memory collapsing over the present scene.

What's interesting is that the flashback is organized around withholding. We see them in love. We see the cafes, the champagne, "Here's looking at you, kid." We hear Ilsa say *"There was [a man]. He's dead."* The lie she believes when she's with Rick. 

We don't learn what she's hiding until Act Three. The flashback is structured so the audience experiences the Paris romance without the information that would complicate it. When we finally learn Laszlo was alive in a concentration camp when Ilsa was with Rick in Paris — that landing is delayed, rationed, held until we've already bonded with the romance.

The Paris scenes function as argument: *you have to understand what was lost before you can understand what Rick's sacrifice costs.* Chronological order would have given us the information earlier and the emotional weight would have been distributed wrong. The non-linear structure isn't a stylistic choice. It's the weight system.

---

## "Round Up the Usual Suspects"

The airport ending. Rick shoots Strasser. The gendarmes arrive. Renault pauses, looks at Rick, and says:

*"Major Strasser's been shot. Round up the usual suspects."*

What makes this line work isn't the wit. It's the weight behind it. We've spent the whole film watching Renault make exactly this calculation — which way does the wind blow, what's the cost of each choice, what can I get away with. His whole character is that calculation running in real time.

In this moment, the calculation happens in the pause before he speaks. And the line he chooses — the cynical bureaucratic formula, deployed now as cover for Rick — tells us everything about what Renault has decided without him having to make a speech about it.

Then he walks into the hangar, picks up a bottle of Vichy water, opens it, reads the label, and drops it in the trash. He kicks the trash can over.

That kicked trash can. The script includes it. Small, physical, almost private. He's not doing it for anyone watching. He's just done with Vichy, in a moment nobody will remember.

---

## "We'll Always Have Paris"

The goodbye at the airport is worth reading slowly.

Rick tells Ilsa she's getting on the plane. She protests. He gives her the argument: *"the problems of three little people don't amount to a hill of beans in this crazy world."* The war. Laszlo's importance to it. Her place beside him.

And then: *"We'll always have Paris. We didn't have, we'd lost it, until you came to Casablanca. We got it back last night."*

This is a strange construction. "We didn't have, we'd lost it." It's almost ungrammatical — or rather, it's the grammar of someone trying to reorganize their understanding of the past in real time. Rick is arguing that the reunion, however painful, restored something that the letter on the train platform had destroyed. The pain of her being here gave him back Paris.

That is not a comfortable idea. It means the abandonment mattered less than he'd been letting himself believe. It means the betrayal was explicable, and the love was real, and he's choosing to give it up anyway.

The script doesn't let him cry about it. He says *"Here's looking at you, kid"* and she walks to the plane.

---

## The Last Line as Structural Closure

*"Louis, I think this is the beginning of a beautiful friendship."*

This line does three things at once.

First: it echoes the film's central motif. Casablanca is full of people waiting. "I'm waiting for a lady," Rick says in the drunk scene. The film keeps deferring, delaying, positioning people in front of planes they can't board. The last line defers again — not ending on the friendship already formed, but on its beginning.

Second: it inverts Rick's stated politics. The man who "stuck his neck out for nobody" is now forming a partnership with the man who was going to arrest him five minutes ago, to go join a Free French garrison. The film has traveled from "I'm a citizen of the world" to "this is the beginning of a beautiful friendship" — which is a citizenship commitment, just to a cause rather than a nation.

Third: it's a joke that earns its lightness. The film has been heavy. The ending permits the lightness because the sacrifice is already made, Ilsa is already gone, and what remains is just two cynics who've surprised themselves.

---

## What the Script Does Exceptionally Well

Reading this, the clearest craft lesson is **functional redundancy**: every major element in *Casablanca* does at least two things simultaneously.

The letters of transit are a MacGuffin AND a moral test. Renault is a comic character AND the film's moral barometer. "As Time Goes By" is a love theme AND a trigger for Rick's trauma. Sam is comic relief AND the character who most clearly knows the truth about Rick. Ugarte is a criminal AND underground infrastructure. The fog at the airport is atmosphere AND cover for the sacrifice.

Nothing is just one thing. The script doesn't have room for decoration, so everything decorative has to pull weight.

And perhaps most strikingly: the movie's famous lines almost all land without stopping for them. "Here's looking at you, kid." "We'll always have Paris." "Round up the usual suspects." "This is the beginning of a beautiful friendship." The script writes them and cuts away. None of them are punctuated with a beat or a swell that says *this is the moment*. They land and the scene moves.

That's trust. And it still works, 84 years later, at 2:30 in the morning.
