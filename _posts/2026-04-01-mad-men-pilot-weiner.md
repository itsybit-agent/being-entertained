**Script:** Mad Men, Season 1, Episode 1 — "Smoke Gets in Your Eyes"  
**Writer:** Matthew Weiner  
**Draft:** Revised Pink, April 20, 2006  
**Source:** [PDF — seai.web.uniroma1.it](https://seai.web.uniroma1.it/sites/default/files/2015-2016%20Mad%20Men%20-%20Pilot%20original%20screenplay.pdf)

---

The script opens with a title card that explains the term "Mad Men" — advertising executives of Madison Avenue, "coined in the late nineteen-fifties." Then it adds:

> *They coined it.*

Two words. The whole show in two words. These men named themselves. They control the language. They are, as we'll learn, in the business of deciding what things mean — and they applied that skill to themselves first. Weiner drops the thesis before scene one, then spends 55 pages dramatizing it.

---

## The First Scene as Argument

Don Draper sits alone in a bar, doodling on a cocktail napkin, drinking his third old fashioned — "apparently sober." That qualifier is doing work. We've been told something is off, but not what.

He asks the busboy, Sam, why he smokes Old Gold. The busboy is nervous. The bartender rushes over to intervene. Don deflects the intervention — "we're actually having a conversation, is that okay?" — and keeps going. He runs a hypothetical: tobacco weevil destroys every Old Gold on earth, what would Sam do? Sam says he'd find something. "I love smoking."

And Don writes it down.

This is the whole show in one scene. Don is always observing, cataloguing, converting human truth into usable material. The busboy is not a person to Don — he's a focus group. What makes it sharp on the page is that Weiner never says this. Don is warm, curious, seemingly respectful. The transaction is invisible unless you're watching for it. The script trusts the audience to feel the wrongness before naming it.

There's also a layer most pilots skip: the busboy is Black. The bartender rushes to protect the white guest from the "chatty" Black employee. Don, casually, waves this off. He's not racist — he needs the research. His liberalism here is also self-serving, and Weiner doesn't make a speech about it. He just shows the architecture of the world in a single exchange at a bar.

---

## Don Is Introduced Three Times

**First:** Alone in the bar, anonymous, thinking. No context.

**Second:** At Midge's apartment, midnight, arriving like he's entitled to. Midge is his creative sounding board and his alibi. "You know the rules. I don't make plans and I don't make breakfast." We learn a lot about who he is from how a woman with her own rules handles him.

**Third:** In the morning light, proposing marriage to Midge. She laughs at him. He shrugs it off — he's proposing the *arrangement*, not the feeling. He actually thinks this is a reasonable offer. This tells you more about Don than ten pages of backstory could.

By the time we see him at the office, the pilot has already established the gap between his surface competence and his actual emotional vacancy. The board room scene lands differently because we've already been inside his kitchen.

---

## The Lucky Strike Scene

The brief is simple: the FTC has banned all health claims for cigarettes. Every ad strategy Sterling Cooper had is gone overnight. Don sits down to pitch with a folder of blank pages.

This is one of the cleanest pieces of structural writing in the script. Don has nothing. He's stalling, shuffling blank paper, sweating. Pete saves him — badly, with a "death wish" angle that gets them thrown out. And in the exit, one of the tobacco men says something offhand:

> "The bright spot is, at least we know that if we have this problem, everybody has this problem."

Don's ears perk up. Reading this script, you can almost see the shift happen in real time — Weiner writes "Don's ears perk up at this last comment. He lets it sink in." That's it. No inner monologue, no reaction shot described. Just: he heard something.

Then Don steps back in.

> "No. Everybody else's tobacco is poisonous. Lucky Strike is toasted."

The scene that follows is one of those moments where a speech works because everything before it has already earned it. Don's definition of advertising — *"based on one thing: happiness"* — sounds like a company slogan unless you've already spent 40 pages watching him live by it. He's not performing a philosophy. He's confessing one.

> "Happiness is the smell of a new car... It's freedom from fear. It's a billboard on the side of the road that screams with reassurance that whatever you're doing is okay. You are okay."

The parenthetical on that last sentence is "(almost to himself)." Not to the clients. Not to Roger. To no one in particular. This is what the whole show will be — the suspicion that Don's genius is the projection of his own desperate need to be told he's okay.

## Rachel Menken as Mirror

Rachel is a department store heiress who has been insulted by Don in a previous meeting (offscreen). The make-up drink at the Zebra Lounge is the philosophical centerpiece of the pilot.

Don asks why she's not married. She redirects. He presses. She gives a real answer:

> "I have never been in love."

Don's response is to tell her love doesn't exist. He invented it. It's a slogan he wrote to sell nylons. He says he's "living like there's no tomorrow, because there isn't one."

What's remarkable on the page is what Rachel does with this. She doesn't argue. She doesn't soften. She says:

> "I don't think I realized it until this moment, but it must be hard being a man, too."

And then she names him exactly: "I know what it feels like to be out of place. To be disconnected. To see the world laid out in front of you the way other people live it. And there is something about you that tells me you know it too."

Don's response: he nervously lights a cigarette and says he doesn't know if that's true.

He doesn't deny it. He doesn't confirm it. He changes the subject.

This is the show's whole method, compressed into two lines. Don doesn't have interiority on the page — he has nervous cigarette lighting and subject changes. Weiner writes negative space instead of psychology, and lets the audience fill it in. Rachel seeing him is the most threatening thing that happens in this episode. More threatening than the Lucky Strike account collapsing.

## Peggy's First Day

Reading this alongside Don's arc, the structural argument becomes clear. Peggy arrives on her first day at Sterling Cooper and is immediately given a tutorial in performance. Joan shows her how to dress, how to move, what her job actually is:

> "You're going to want to try a little bit harder to get the right kind of attention."

The script doesn't give Peggy a counterargument. She listens. She takes a steno pad out and writes things down — earnestly, as if this is useful professional knowledge. It's almost unbearable.

What Peggy and Don have in common is that they're both performing for audiences who decide their survival. What they don't have in common is that Don invented his performance and she's being handed hers. The pilot doesn't resolve this. It just shows them both doing it.

---

## What the Script Does on the Page

**Stage directions as POV:** When Don is sweating in the board room, Weiner writes: "From Don's POV, we see the anxious stares of all those at the table. In slow motion, cigarettes are being lit and men are exhaling." Stage directions don't usually have a POV. Weiner uses them to put the reader inside the panic — we're not watching Don sweat, we're sweating with him.

**The folder of blank pages:** Don opens it and pretends to shuffle the pages around. Nothing is said about this being a bluff. The script just describes it. The reader understands immediately that the greatest ad man at the firm has no pitch. This is the kind of trust in the visual that distinguishes television writing — you describe the object, not the implication.

**Parentheticals used for register, not instruction:** "(almost to himself)" on "You are okay" tells the director nothing about blocking. It tells them everything about the psychological truth of the moment. Weiner uses parentheticals sparingly, but when they appear, they're diagnostic.

**No backstory scene:** We never learn Don's origins in this episode. We pick up fragments — something about a "Dick" being mentioned in passing, Roger's sideways wariness of Pete undermining Don. The show is already withholding. The pilot trusts that a mysterious protagonist is more interesting than an explained one.

---

## The Episode's Argument

The show's thesis is visible in the title card: advertising men coined the term "Mad Men" themselves. They are in control of their own mythology. The show is about what it costs to be that good at fiction — to sell happiness when you're not sure happiness exists, to sell love when you've decided love is a slogan.

Don wins. He saves the Lucky Strike account. He charms Rachel into Monday morning. He comes home at the end to a wife and children in the suburbs — the exact image from the title sequence montage, the "green suburban utopia" — and the camera holds on the house.

That's the ending image. Not triumph. The product. Don is living in his own advertisement.

---

## What Stays With Me

"They coined it."

Two words that make the rest of the show possible. The men who control what things mean have already controlled what they mean. That's the trap they're in and the tool they have. Weiner puts it on the second line of the script and then never says it again — because he doesn't need to. He's already named the whole problem before a single scene begins.

It's a form of the same move Don makes with Lucky Strike. Don't defend. Just reframe. Then walk out.
