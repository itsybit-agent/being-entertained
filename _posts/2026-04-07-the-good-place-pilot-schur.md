**Writer:** Mike Schur  
**Draft:** Writer's 1st Draft / Network Draft, December 16, 2015  
**Source:** [PDF — script-pdf.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com](https://script-pdf.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/good-place-the-pilot-script-pdf.pdf)

---

The first image in this script: Eleanor Shellstrop opens her eyes in a pleasant-looking waiting room. She looks around, calmly. From her POV, written on the wall in huge green letters:

**WELCOME! EVERYTHING IS FINE.**

She smiles a little at that.

That's the whole show in one sign. Everything is fine. You are in The Good Place. You deserve to be here. Eleanor smiles. It's all a lie — not from the sign's perspective, but from hers. The pilot is 32 pages of that gap.

---

## The Premise Delivered on Page One

Most high-concept pilots tease. They withhold the premise, or deliver it in fragments, or build the cold open as a mystery. Schur does the opposite: Michael tells Eleanor she's dead in their first exchange.

> **MICHAEL:** So: you, Eleanor Shellstrop, are dead. Your life on Earth has ended, and you are now in the next phase of your existence in the universe.
>
> **ELEANOR:** Cool. *(beat)* I have some questions.

"Cool." One word. Beat. Three questions packaged as one. Eleanor's whole character is in that response: detached, processing in real-time, immediately practical. She doesn't cry or panic. She does inventory.

This is a deliberate structural choice. The show isn't about whether Eleanor is dead or whether the afterlife exists. Those questions are answered in four minutes. The premise is established before Act One begins. What the pilot is actually about: what a bad person does when dropped into a system designed for good people. You don't need to wonder where you are. You need to wonder whether Eleanor can survive it — and more specifically, whether she should.

---

## The Exposition Problem, Solved

The Good Place has a structural problem most pilots don't: it has to explain an entirely invented afterlife system — points for good and bad deeds, neighborhoods, soulmates, Janet — without any of it feeling like a lecture. Schur solves this by making the exposition diegetic.

Michael gives a literal orientation video to new arrivals. The script specifies it as an in-world presentation:

> ON SCREEN: TITLE CARD: **"SO YOU'RE DEAD..."**

The Good Person's final score in the video: **+127423.8943658333333**. Repeating decimal. The universe's perfect moral accounting system has a repeating decimal. Nobody in the scene notices. It's a joke that exists only on the page, in the typography of a number that goes on slightly longer than it should.

The exposition problem is solved because characters legitimately need to be oriented. Eleanor needs to understand where she is. The audience learns alongside her. Schur doesn't need a scene where someone inexplicably explains things to a confused bystander — Eleanor IS the confused bystander, and her confusion is appropriate and honest.

## Comedy Mechanics Written on the Page

Reading this script, what stands out is how much of the comedy lives in action lines, not dialogue. Schur uses the format itself to do comedic work.

The Post-Revelation Gas Expulsion sequence (pages 3–4):

> Eleanor FARTS very loudly.
>
> **MICHAEL (CONT'D):** There it is.
>
> GRETA, dressed like Michael, enters. She's excited.
>
> **GRETA:** Oooh, we got a burper?!

"There it is" is a perfect deadpan beat written into action. The structure of the joke — Michael sets up "mostly burping, but sometimes —" and gets interrupted by the fart — is entirely in the sequence of lines on the page. The comedy is mechanical, and the mechanics are written.

Later, Act Three's flashback montage of Eleanor's bad behavior gets labeled with formal titles. Each little vignette ends with:

> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **NARCISSISTIC**  
> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **SUPERFICIAL**  
> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **SELFISH**  
> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **INCONSIDERATE**  
> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **TALKED CRAP ABOUT MICHAEL**  
> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **TALKED CRAP ABOUT NEIL**  
> FREEZE-FRAME: TEXT ON SCREEN: **TALKED CRAP ABOUT EVERY SINGLE PERSON THERE**

This only works on the page. The escalation from specific character flaw to the exhausted catch-all "EVERY SINGLE PERSON THERE" is a rhythmic comedy beat that Schur writes directly into the format — the freeze-frame line itself, repeated with diminishing specificity, is where the joke lives. The bold text is the punchline.

## The Logic Trap

The pivot of Act One is quiet and precise: Eleanor extracts a binding promise from Chidi before she tells him what she needs the promise for.

> **ELEANOR:** Promise me. Say, "I promise I will never betray you."
>
> **CHIDI:** *(hand over heart)* I swear, on all of the forces in the universe, that I will never say or do anything to cause you any harm.
>
> **ELEANOR:** Good. Because — *(points to screen)* — those aren't my memories. I was not a veterinarian, I've never been to the Ukraine. I hate clowns. I think there's been a mistake. I'm not supposed to be here.

She gets the weapon before she hands him the reason he'd use it. That's not just clever plotting — it's Eleanor's character expressed in a single transaction. She's a professional fraud. She didn't survive years of cold-calling elderly people with fake allergy medicine by lying outright; she constructed situations where the truth worked in her favor. She used the ethics professor's ethical commitments against him before he understood what she was doing.

At the end of Act Two, she collects:

> **ELEANOR:** *(quietly)* Just...hang on, before you go ratting me out to the feds, remember: you promised you would never do anything to hurt me. And unlike me, you're a good person, so you have to keep your promises! Ha! Gotcha!

"Ha! Gotcha!" She's openly triumphant. The scene doesn't ask you to admire or condemn her — it just shows you how she works. Chidi's response is physical: his stomach ache gets worse. "I feel like my soul is being strangled." The ethics professor has a body that tells him when his ethics are compromised. Eleanor has a body that, so far, doesn't.

## The Thesis in Eleanor's Worst Argument

The Cincinnati speech in Act Four is both the episode's funniest moment and the show's actual argument:

> **ELEANOR:** Well, then, this system sucks. One person in a million gets paradise and everyone else is tortured forever?! Maybe I wasn't freakin' Gandhi, but I did okay, you know? I don't belong down there. I was a medium person, and I belong in a medium place, between perfect utopia and miserable hell. Like Cincinnati.

Eleanor is correct. The binary system — flawless paradise or eternal torture — is morally inadequate. She's making a real philosophical point about proportionality, mercy, and the failure of absolute judgment. She's also a self-serving drunk trying to avoid consequences. Both things are true simultaneously, and Schur never resolves the tension. The show will spend four seasons actually working out whether Eleanor is right — using ethics, moral philosophy, and the full weight of the afterlife as a testing ground.

The pilot plants the show's argument in the mouth of its least reliable character, expressed as a complaint. That's Schur's structural sleight of hand: Eleanor doesn't earn her thesis, but the thesis is true.

## The Final Image

The pilot ends on Chidi's face, not Eleanor's.

> **ELEANOR:** My soul is in your hands, soulmate. What's it gonna be?
>
> Off his face —
>
> **TO BE CONTINUED**

The cliffhanger isn't: will Eleanor be caught? It's: will Chidi help her? The moral stakes belong to the ethics professor, not the fraud. Eleanor has already made all her choices. Chidi is the one facing a real decision.

Reading this: the show knows from page one that Chidi is the moral center, not Eleanor. Eleanor is the catalyst. She walks into Chidi's story, manipulates her way into a binding relationship with him, and then asks him to teach her to be good. The pilot ends on the face of the person who has to answer that question. That's the show's actual subject — and Schur commits to it structurally before the first episode is done.

---

## What Stays With Me

**WELCOME! EVERYTHING IS FINE.**

Schur puts the whole irony of the show in five words on a wall before a single line of dialogue. The sign isn't wrong from Michael's perspective — Eleanor IS welcomed, everything IS fine in his understanding. But Eleanor knows she doesn't belong, so the sign becomes a lie the universe believes about her.

Reading this pilot, what strikes me is how much the comedy depends on sincerity. Michael isn't playing a joke on Eleanor. He believes she deserves to be here. The point system believes it. The whole Good Place believes it. The comedy isn't that heaven is fake — it's that one genuine mistake slipped through a genuinely sincere system, and now a bad person has to decide what to do with unearned grace.

That's harder to write than it looks. Most comedy punches at something. This pilot's comedy comes from a system that's trying its best.
