The script opens with Abigail falling face-first into the mud. This is not a setup for comedy. It's a precise structural image that the whole script mirrors at the end.

By the last scene, Abigail is on her knees rubbing the Queen's inflamed legs. Anne — sick, barely lucid, losing the use of one arm — reaches out and grabs a fist of Abigail's hair. Holds it. Closes her eyes. Across the room, seventeen white rabbits stare at Abigail from their cage.

The fall into mud and the hair in a fist are the same image. What changed is the direction: one is an arrival, one is an arrival that doesn't know it's a trap.

---

## Who Is This About

Reading this script, I kept trying to assign the centre. Most films have one.

It opens with Abigail — her carriage arrival, her humiliation, her cunning. The first act belongs to her. Then it becomes Sarah's script: her dominance, her control of the Queen, her confidence so absolute it reads as invulnerability. Then Anne's: her grief, her illness, her seventeen rabbits. Then back to Abigail as she manoeuvres Sarah out entirely.

The title is a red herring. There is no single "favourite" — the word names a position that gets filled and refilled. The script's subject is the position itself: what it costs to hold it, what it does to the person who wants it.

Davis and McNamara never tell you who to mourn. That's not ambiguity as a style choice. It's the argument.

---

## Dialogue as Dominance Test

Almost no exchange in this script is informational. Conversations are power negotiations wearing the clothes of conversations.

The first meeting between Sarah and Abigail — mud-covered, fly-attended, mortifying — goes:

> **SARAH:** A monster for the children to play with perhaps?
>
> **ABIGAIL:** Yes. If you like.
>
> She raises her hands and makes a 'Gggrrr' sound. They smile at this.

Abigail doesn't beg. She escalates to comply, which is a different move — taking the insult and weaponising it as charm. Sarah notices. The scene turns.

Later, when Harley calls Sarah a "Cunt" in a political meeting:

> **SARAH:** And there I was expecting something witty that rhymed.

No flinch. The response doesn't defend her — it dismisses him for failing to be clever enough. In one line, she's established that the insult didn't land, that she expected better, and that his best attack was inadequate. It's a three-move political counter in eight words.

The script is full of this. Even Anne's most vulnerable moments are negotiations. The "Are you still there?" exchange:

> **ANNE:** Are you still there?
>
> **SARAH:** Yes.
>
> *Beat.*
>
> **ANNE:** Still?
>
> **SARAH:** Yes.

Two people establishing that one will not leave the other alone. It's tender and also a test with a pass/fail.

## The Language Refuses to Be Historical

The characters speak in a way that collapses period-costume distance.

Sarah tells Anne: "You look like a badger." Harley tells a colleague to keep his duck away from him or he'll "pull his liver out and eat it with a cornichon." Abigail, being solicited as a spy by Harley, responds: "You want something. To fuck me?"

This isn't anachronism as comedy gimmick. It's a structural argument: the power games being played in the early 1700s English court are not historical curiosities. They're continuous. The script refuses to let you hold them at arm's length as "things people used to do." The language pulls everything into the present tense.

What this requires from the reader — from anyone approaching this material — is a refusal of the safety of period distance. The Favourite is a film about how people who have something exploit people who don't, and how people who don't have something will do whatever it takes to get it, and how power turns every relationship into leverage. The costumes are costumes. The behavior is now.

---

## Action Lines as Anti-Baroque

Here's the establishing shot of the palace — a place of extraordinary opulence, the seat of English power during the War of Spanish Succession, home to a queen and her courtiers in full period regalia:

> *A man carves bloody slices off a roasting elk (CARVING MAN), its head still intact.*

That's it. One line. No atmosphere, no grandeur, no camera-eye sweep. Just a man carving a dead elk with the head still on.

Davis and McNamara write action lines in the same tone throughout: spare, observational, zero editorial comment. When Harley trips Abigail on the garden path and she goes "face first into the stony path," the stage direction doesn't tell us how to feel about it. When Mrs Meg strikes Abigail's bare back with the birch, the action reads: "Mrs Meg pulls her arm back, strikes her again." Factual. Clinical.

The contrast between the baroque setting and the stripped-down description is an argument about the setting. All the finery is just decoration on the elk. The birch still hurts. The power still operates the same way.

## One Scene, Seventeen Rabbits

The most economical piece of characterisation in the script:

Anne is showing Abigail her rabbit enclosure. Seventeen white rabbits.

> **ANNE:** He likes you. I lost some seventeen children. Some born as blood, some without breath, and some just briefly with me.
>
> *Abigail's heart instantly goes out to her, tears pop from her eyes taking Anne by surprise.*
>
> **ANNE (CONT'D):** Oh my dear.
>
> *Then Anne cries.*

That's it. One scene. One revelation. Never repeated or expanded.

But every previous rabbit scene retroactively becomes grief. And every subsequent rabbit scene — Anne feeding them, watching them, letting them loose on the bed — carries weight the script never has to restate. The rabbits are always all seventeen dead children, in every shot, for the rest of the script, without any line of dialogue doing that work again.

The restraint is the craft. You tell it once. You trust the audience to carry it.

## The Gun Scene as Structural Prophecy

Sarah shoots Abigail with what appears to be a loaded pistol. Abigail falls. Then:

> **SARAH:** If you forget to load the pellet the gun fires, makes the sound, but releases no shot. It is a great jape do you agree?
>
> **ABIGAIL:** Yes.. great.
>
> **SARAH:** Maybe we will think of some use for it one day. Sometimes it is hard to remember if you have loaded the pellet or not. I do fear confusion and accidents.
>
> **ABIGAIL:** I'm sure people will be careful.

The script frames this as dark comedy. It also tells you exactly what's coming. The whole dynamic between Sarah and Abigail is: both of them holding guns, neither certain which is loaded. Each exchange where Abigail smiles at Sarah is a possible gun — warmth or calculation, can't tell. Each scene where Sarah teaches Abigail something (to shoot, to navigate the court) might be loaded in Sarah's favour or Abigail's.

"Sometimes it is hard to remember if you have loaded the pellet or not." That line is the thesis of the film, delivered as a joke between two women one of whom is about to betray the other.

---

## The Dead Wren

Harley tries to recruit Abigail as a spy. She refuses. He trips her, lets her fall, then trips her again by offering his hand and releasing it. He threatens her. He leaves.

> *Abigail sees the little wren, lying dead on the ground beside the path.*

The script doesn't explain it. The wren was presumably there the whole time. Davis and McNamara put it in the action line without comment and never mention it again.

There's no assigned meaning. It's just a dead bird on the path after Harley's exit. The reader has to decide whether to read it as omen, as accident, as commentary, or as nothing. The script offers zero guidance.

I read it as a dead wren.

---

## Sarah's Thesis, Delivered Through a Locked Door

Sarah has been expelled from court. Anne, through a closed door between them, won't open it. Sarah pushes through the locked wood:

> **SARAH:** She does not love you!
>
> **ANNE:** Because how could anyone? She wants nothing from me! Unlike you.
>
> **SARAH:** She wants nothing from you, and yet somehow she is a Lady with two thousand a year...
>
> **ANNE:** I wish you would love me as she does.
>
> **SARAH:** You wish me to lie to you?! 'Oh you look like an angel that fell from heaven your Majesty'. No! Some days you look like a badger. And you can rely on me to tell you. 
>
> **ANNE:** Why?!
>
> **SARAH:** Because I will not lie! That is love!

The thesis is right. Abigail's affection is performance; Sarah's is the genuine, difficult, unacceptable kind that tells you when you look like a badger because someone has to. The script gives Sarah the correct argument. Then it gives Anne the rebuttal — not a counter-argument, but a preference:

> **ANNE:** I must go attend to my duties.

She walks out. The correct argument loses. Not because it's wrong. Because it is exhausting to be loved honestly when someone else is offering easy warmth instead.

The script doesn't moralize this. It just shows what Anne chooses and what it costs.

---

## The Last Image

Abigail is drunk. She stumbled into the Queen's chambers, vomited in a vase, fell asleep. Now Anne — barely functional, one arm limp, struggling with papers she can't parse — slides off the bed to the floor, in pain. Abigail rushes to help her. Anne says: "How dare you touch the Queen like that."

Then:

> **ANNE:** My leg. Rub it.

Abigail gets it, drops to her knees. Anne grabs at Abigail's hair. Holds a fist of it.

> *Abigail rubs, looks over at the cage full of rabbits across the room sitting staring at her.*

The rabbits. In their cage. Staring back at the person who took the position.

Abigail arrived in mud, was birched, was humiliated, climbed, manipulated, won. She is now on her knees being used in a room full of caged grief, staring back at her. This is the top. This is what the top looks like from the inside. The fall was at the beginning. The trap closed at the end.

Davis and McNamara don't editorialize the last image. They just put Abigail and the rabbits in the same room and let them look at each other.

**THE END.**

---

## What Stays With Me

The script gives Sarah the best lines, the sharpest politics, the genuine love. It then removes her from the story with clinical efficiency, and Abigail watches, and the script ends on Abigail in the position Sarah held.

What this means: the position was never about the person. The title is The Favourite, not The Person Who Won. The position demands performance. It consumes the person who performs it. Sarah knew this — it's why she said what she said through the door. It didn't help her.

Reading this, I kept thinking about what it means to write a script where the most competent person loses. Sarah isn't naive. She isn't outplayed through stupidity. She's outplayed because she refused to become what the position required. Abigail has no such restriction.

The rabbits are still in their cage at the end. Nobody let them out.
