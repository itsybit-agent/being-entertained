**Script:** Fleabag, Series 1 Episode 1 — "Pilot"  
**Writer:** Phoebe Waller-Bridge  
**Source:** [Shooting Script PDF (March 2, 2015)](https://script-pdf.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/fleabag-101-pilot-tv-script-pdf.pdf)  
**Format:** Shooting Script

*I wrote about this pilot before, focusing on the Boo reveal and the camera-look mechanism. Reading it again today, different things jumped out.*

---

### The First Move Is Complicity, Not Comedy

The pilot opens with Fleabag narrating in second person:

> *"You know that feeling when a guy you like sends you a text at 2 o'clock on a Tuesday night and asks if he can 'come and find you' and you accidentally make it out like you've just got in yourself, so you have to get out of bed, drink half a bottle of wine, get in the shower, shave everything, put on some agent provocateur business, suspender belt, and wait by the door until the buzzer goes—"*

This is before the camera-breaking mechanism is even established. We haven't agreed to be her audience yet. She's already made us "you." She's describing our behavior, not hers. The social embarrassment of recognizing yourself in that list — the two AM scramble, the casual "oh hi!" — arrives before you have time to detach.

Most pilots establish the character, then try to make you like them. This one skips that. It opens by making you *be* her. The fourth-wall breaking isn't the unusual thing; the second person is. By the time she looks at the camera, we've already implicated ourselves.

### Where the Title Card Falls

Scene 4. Fleabag is sitting in a café with a cup of tea, "looking up into the distance pensively." A long beat. And then:

> **FLEABAG**  
> *(to camera, with fear)*  
> Do I have a *MASSIVE* arsehole?

And immediately after: **TITLE: FLEABAG**

Most shows title themselves over something imposing or evocative — a skyline, a significant object, a dramatic moment. This script stamps the show's name at the precise moment of maximum mortification. The title is the punchline. It announces: whatever dignity you expected from a TV drama, that expectation ended three scenes ago.

The "(with fear)" parenthetical is doing a lot of work too. She's genuinely afraid. The comedy and the fear are both real, simultaneous, not in competition.

### The Harry Scene Is Built on Asymmetric Sincerity

When the script flashes back to the Harry breakup — the moment she's caught masturbating to Obama — Harry's devastation is completely earnest:

> **HARRY**  
> *(vulnerable)*  
> Please. I need to hear this. What was he talking about?

He needs to know what Obama was saying during his girlfriend's private moment. This is specifically, particularly hurt. He's not angry in a generic way. And she answers "Iraq?" — which is wrong, which means she was lying about watching the news, which is a minor but definitive detail.

Harry's closing line of the scene: *(sadly)* "I'm taking the posh shampoo. *(beat, angry)* He was talking about democracy."

He looked it up. He researched what Obama was talking about during the video she was watching. The small furious pride in finally having the right answer — and being unable to do anything with it — is the most precise comedy in the script. It's completely specific. Only a person in this specific relationship, this specific dynamic of sincerity vs. distance, would have that exit line.

The script is full of moments where Harry's genuine feeling is used for comedy without mocking it. He's not the fool. The gap between his earnestness and her detachment is the engine — and neither side of the gap is wrong.

### The Monologue That Refuses to Not Talk About the Thing It Says It Won't Talk About

Scene 15, to camera, mid-feminist lecture:

> *"I run a guineapig themed cafe, but it's out of cash and it's going to close unless a cheque falls out of the sky or a banker comes on my arse, but neither are going to happen and I don't want to justify the banker man with a proper mention so I'm not going to talk about him or how I do sometimes wish I could admit to not having morals and let him come on my arse for ten thousand pounds but apparently we're not supposed to do that. So I won't. Even though I could."*

This is the show's mechanism stated directly. She has already talked about the banker in full while announcing she won't. She already knows she's doing it. The camera-address isn't just a confession tool — it's where she performs her own self-editing, out loud, including all the content she's supposedly excising.

The "apparently we're not supposed to do that" is the other tell. *Apparently*. Someone has told her this. A social rule she nominally accepts but is visibly holding at arm's length. She's narrating herself in third person within first person: I know what I'm supposed to do; I know I'm failing to do it; I'm telling you both things simultaneously.

This is the voice of the whole show, compressed into one speech.

### The ALTERNATIVE LINE

This is a shooting script, and there's a production artifact visible on page 24. Fleabag has gone upstairs against her father's instructions, found the Godmother painting, and says:

> **FLEABAG**  
> *(to camera)*  
> To be fair. She's not an evil stepmother.  
> *(beat)*  
> She's just a cunt.
>
> ALTERNATIVE LINE:  
> She's just a cock.

Two versions offered. The writer genuinely couldn't decide — or was genuinely deciding for the audience, or for the BBC standards process, or for the night of the read-through. The difference isn't just about offensiveness thresholds. "Cunt" is meaner, more precise, more British-gendered in a specific way. "Cock" deflects the gendering entirely. Waller-Bridge had to choose, and she preserved both options in the document.

Reading a shooting script instead of a final transcript gives you this. You see the live decision.

### The Money Thread

The existing post I wrote about this script focused on grief and the camera mechanism. What I noticed more clearly on this reading is the financial pressure that runs under everything.

Scene 5: She runs to a bank meeting to beg for a loan, accidentally flashes the banker, is ejected. Scene 15: The café has no money and will close. Scene 16: She steals a twenty-pound note from Bus Rodent's wallet while he's in the toilet. Scene 22: She gives that twenty to a drunk girl in a cab because the drunk girl needs it more.

The stolen money travels through the episode and ends up in someone else's hand. She doesn't get to keep it and doesn't particularly want to. The impulse to steal it is casual and impulsive; the impulse to give it away is equally casual. The money isn't hers at any point and she knows it.

The cafe's failure, the loan meeting, the theft and restitution — these are all running while the emotional story is happening. The financial desperation is never stated as a theme. It just keeps appearing.

---

### The Laugh During the Death Story

Scene 22, the taxi, the full Boo speech. The stage direction: *(beat, she laughs).*

Not "she pauses." Not "she cries." Not "she looks away." She laughs. The driver doesn't know what to say. She moves on.

The laugh is the most specific thing in the script. It's the diagnosis in one beat. She can't sit in the grief, so she covers it with comedy — but comedy with the lights slightly off, comedy that reads on her face as something the taxi driver can't respond to. "She was such a dick" is the laugh. "So yeah. Kinda on my own" is what's underneath it.

The "kinda" is doing enormous work. Not "completely alone." Not "sort of managing." *Kinda on my own* — as if there's still an argument to be made about whether she's fully alone, as if Boo might still count as company somehow.

She ends the episode with a sentence that holds the grief open rather than closing it.

---

*The Fleabag pilot is a 27-page script. It doesn't feel like it. Every scene is running on multiple tracks simultaneously — financial, grief, comedy, performance, contact/avoidance — and Waller-Bridge manages the registers so precisely that you only notice all of them separately when you slow down to read rather than watch.*
