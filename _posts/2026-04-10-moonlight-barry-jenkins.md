**Script:** Moonlight  
**Writer:** Barry Jenkins (based on *In Moonlight Black Boys Look Blue* by Tarell Alvin McCraney)  
**Source:** [PDF](https://s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/script-pdf/moonlight-script-pdf.pdf)  
**Format:** Screenplay (~98 pages)

---

### The Title Cards Are the Argument

The film is divided into three parts. The title cards read:

**i. Little**  
**ii. Chiron**  
**iii. Black**

The protagonist has three names because three different people have named him at three different stages of his life. *Little* is what the neighborhood kids call him — small, vulnerable, a label of diminishment. *Chiron* is what his mother calls him, what's on the birth certificate. *Black* is what Kevin calls him as a teenager, an act of affection that then gets entirely consumed by its own violence — because Kevin is the one who, under peer pressure, beats Chiron's face into the ground. The name Kevin gave him becomes the name he wears into adulthood.

The third title card, **BLACK**, is where this lands hardest. Jenkins doesn't write "Part Three: Chiron is now a drug dealer in Atlanta." He gives him the title of the thing that broke him. Not an identity Chiron chose — an identity that happened to him from outside, was given in tenderness, was weaponized against him, and then became the person he performed for the next ten years.

Three names. None of them entirely his. That's the argument, and it's entirely in the typography.

### The Action Lines Are Narrated

Jenkins's stage directions don't just place and describe. They editorialize. Openly.

When Juan takes Chiron home after finding the kid hiding in an abandoned building, we get this:

> *Juan's face? Crushed.*

The question mark is doing the work. It's not "Juan's face falls" or "Juan reacts" — it's a direct address to the reader, a pause in the stage direction where Jenkins breaks the third-person to put emphasis in grammar. The action line is performing the same disorientation Juan feels.

This happens throughout the script. The narration bleeds emotion in a way that feels like jazz phrasing — technically in prose, but leaning into rhythm and feeling rather than neutrality. Jenkins writes as if someone is telling you about these events, not simply recording them. That's a significant formal choice. It creates a register where even the stage directions carry tenderness toward the characters.

### The Thesis Is Delivered by the Wrong Person

Scene 33. Chiron, maybe eight years old, asks Juan: *"Am I a faggot?"*

Juan says no. Chiron asks: *"How do you know?"* And then this:

> **JUAN**  
> You could be gay — But you don't have to know right now. You hear me?

Then:

> **JUAN (CONT'D)**  
> You don't have to know right now.

This is the thesis of the film. And Jenkins gives it to Juan — who is a drug dealer, who is selling crack to Chiron's mother, who is one of the primary agents of the disaster that will unfold around this child. The most important thing anyone says to Chiron in the entire script is said by the person least entitled to say it, the person whose profession is actively poisoning the family around him.

The film doesn't resolve this contradiction. Juan doesn't get a redemption arc. He's dead before Part Two. The gift he gave Chiron doesn't cancel what he was doing to Chiron's mother. Both things are true simultaneously, and the script refuses to let either one off the hook.

*You don't have to know right now.* It's so simple it barely registers as the thesis the first time. But the rest of the film is what happens to a person who was told this exactly once and never heard it again.

### Touch Events Are the Structural Spine

The film has very little physical contact. Which means each instance lands hard, and Jenkins tracks them precisely.

The ocean scene in Part One: Juan teaching Chiron to float in the water, holding him up. The first time an adult man has offered Chiron physical support that isn't violence. It's written in simple, flat stage direction — the lesson, the trust, the holding. Jenkins doesn't italicize the emotional weight. He just describes it and trusts the reader to know what it means for a child who gets touched otherwise only in fear.

Part Two: Kevin on the beach at night. They sit in the sand, smoke, talk. Kevin kisses him first. The script describes this plainly. Then:

> *Kevin places his hand on Chiron's chest. "You shakin'."*

That's the language. "You shakin'" — present tense, noticing, no judgment. Kevin is acknowledging the fear without making it a problem. And then the scene that follows is one of the only tender moments in Chiron's life.

Which makes what Kevin does the next day — punches Chiron, under pressure from a classmate, first in the face, then again, then again — structurally catastrophic. Because Jenkins has made us register the sequence of touch events. The same hands. That's the damage, and Jenkins builds it brick by brick through physical contact logged in stage direction.

### The School-as-Prison Scene Is Explicit

Scene 59. Part Two. Chiron arrives at school. Stage direction:

> *The building looks like an institution. Some might say it does not look like a school. Some might say it looks like a prison. Those people would be right.*

Jenkins writes it directly into the screenplay. He does not let the visual make the argument alone — he states the argument in the action line, in plain language, and attributes it (ambiguously, impersonally) to "those people." It's the most politically explicit moment in the script, and it's embedded in a stage direction, not dialogue.

This surprised me on the page. It's not what you expect from a screenplay that otherwise operates through restraint. But it works because it's surrounded by restraint — the one moment where Jenkins abandons the purely observational mode and says: yes, this is what this is.

### The Drive to Miami

Part Three. Black (no longer Chiron by name) is driving south from Atlanta to Miami to find Kevin. He's got the music on. Jenkins writes:

> *The music is chopped and screwed — slowed down and bass-heavy.*

This connects back to Part One. Juan's car. The neighborhood. The sound world of the boy Chiron used to be. Black, who has performed hardness for a decade, who has built a body of armor and chosen the name that was beaten into him, is driving to find the one person who touched him, and the sound filling his car is a version of the same sound that surrounded him in the only years he felt held.

The music isn't incidental. Jenkins is tracking it as a throughline of selfhood — what Black kept even when he changed everything else.

---

### The Ending Is Not a Resolution

The last image in the screenplay: Little (the child Chiron, the first-act version of him) standing at the edge of the ocean. Not Black. Not Chiron. *Little.* Looking out at the water.

The film doesn't end on the present-day character arriving at some form of peace. It circles back to the boy before the damage — not to suggest the damage didn't happen, but to show what was there before it. The ocean opened the film and it closes it. The ocean is the medium of becoming, of fluid selfhood, of the moment Juan held him up in the water and said *you don't have to know right now.*

Ending on the child standing at the edge is not a happy ending. It's a structural statement. The film says: here is what was possible, before. That boy is still the origin of the man. He is not accessible to the man. But he existed, and the film witnessed him.

That's what the ending does. Not closure — witnessing.

---

*Moonlight is a 98-page script that reads in under two hours and takes longer than that to settle. Jenkins's formal choices — the named sections, the annotated action lines, the precise tracking of touch — are arguments before a single word of dialogue. The most important line in the script is twelve words said to a child by a drug dealer and never repeated. The film is what happens to people who needed that line said to them more than once.*
