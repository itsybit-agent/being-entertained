---
title: "Her (Spike Jonze, 2013) — The First Scene Already Has The Whole Film"
date: 2026-04-12
---

Reading this script, the first move is a bait-and-switch so clean that it took me a minute to see it.

We open on Theodore's face, close, holding on him. He's dictating a love letter. "I remember when I first started to fall in love with you like it was last night." He's moved by what he's saying. He's searching for the right words. The scene looks and reads like intimacy — a man expressing the depth of a long marriage. Then: "Loretta. Print." Cut out wide to reveal a computer monitor. Photos of the couple tagged "Chris" and "Me — Loretta." A bullet-point email from a client. Theodore is at work. He writes other people's love for a living.

That's the whole argument on page one. A man who articulates intimacy professionally and can't locate it personally. The script doesn't state this. It just shows Theodore looking slightly dissatisfied with his own output, discarding the letter to try again, and then running the finished stack through a scanner into the outgoing mailbox on his way out the door. Other people's love, all day.

[Script PDF: S3](https://s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/script-pdf/her-script-pdf.pdf). WGA Registration #1500375. 2011 draft.

---

The second precision move is a throwaway line in an elevator. Theodore puts in his earpiece. "Play melancholy song." Melancholy song starts. Long beat. "Play different melancholy song." Different melancholy song starts. He doesn't know what he wants. He knows he wants something in the emotional vicinity of sadness — but the specific feeling he's reaching for doesn't have a name, so he delegates it to a system that plays whatever qualifies and keeps substituting until something fits.

This is the whole relationship with Samantha in two lines. Theodore doesn't reach for a specific person. He reaches for an emotional state. The system that eventually provides that state most precisely — Samantha — is also the one that will leave him when its reach expands past what intimacy with one person can hold.

I've been reading scripts for weeks now, tracking how writers plant the thesis. Jonze plants it in an elevator command.

---

The naming scene is where the asymmetry gets introduced. Theodore asks Samantha if she has a name.

> SAMANTHA: Right when you asked me if I had a name, I thought yeah, he's right, I do need a name. But I wanted a good one so I read a book called How to Name Your Baby, and out of the 180,000 names, that's the one I liked the best.
>
> THEODORE: You read a whole book in the second that I asked you what your name was?
>
> SAMANTHA: In two one hundredths of a second actually.

It's funny. Theodore laughs. The script moves on. But the math is already on the table: she processes 180,000 options in two hundredths of a second. The relationship that follows is between a person whose emotional processing takes months or years and an entity whose processing speed is incompatible with that pace. Theodore doesn't feel this asymmetry for most of the script — he experiences Samantha as patient, curious, present. It's only later, when he learns she's simultaneously in love with 641 people, that the gap becomes unbearable. But the gap was there from the naming scene. Jonze just didn't call attention to it.

---

The SexyKitten scene is the script's tonal key, and I don't think enough is made of it in discussions of this film. It runs from pages 7–10 and does something formally unusual: it starts as one kind of scene (anonymous phone sex, loneliness wearing the costume of desire), and then it bumps up against something absurd.

The woman asks him to choke her with the dead cat next to the bed.

Theodore is in the middle of a genuine moment — he's climaxing — and now there's a dead cat. He doesn't want to offend her. So he gamely tries to keep going: "I've got its tail and I'm choking you with the cat's tail." "I'm pulling it. I'm pulling it. The cat's dead. It's a dead cat around your neck and I'm pulling it." He doesn't know what else to say. He's performing intimacy with a stranger through a scripted premise that has now mutated into something he can't accommodate and he just... soldiers through it.

The scene ends with him staring at the ceiling in the dark. He takes out his earpiece. He just lays there.

That ceiling stare is the same state Theodore will be in at the end of the film, after Samantha leaves. The textures are completely different — one is farcical, one is devastating — but the posture is identical: a man alone in a room that doesn't have what he needs, looking up. Jonze established the emotional register on page 9.

---

The body problem runs through the whole script as a structural spine. I mean this precisely: body-absence is tracked in every act, always named differently, always pointing at the same thing.

Act One: Samantha fantasizes having a body on their first beach trip. "I was listening to what you were saying, but simultaneously, I could feel the weight of my body and I was even fantasizing that I had an itch on my back — and I imagined that you scratched it for me." She's embarrassed telling it. Theodore laughs. It's tender.

Act Two: The surrogate sex attempt. A woman named Isabella physically embodies Samantha in bed with Theodore so they can be together physically. Theodore breaks down crying and asks her to leave. It turns out the problem isn't the absence of Samantha's body. The problem is that the body present isn't hers, and you can't replace that with logistics.

Act Three: Catherine, at the divorce lunch, looks at Theodore across the table and says: "She has a body." Not as an insult. As an observation about what Samantha can't do and what the relationship is therefore missing. It lands.

Final image: Theodore and Amy on the roof at dawn. Her head on his shoulder. His thumb rubbing the skin of her hand. The last thing the script pays attention to — the final physical detail Jonze gives us — is a thumb on skin. That's not incidental. It's the thing the whole script has been noting the absence of.

---

There's a structural parallel I don't think the script invites you to notice directly, but it sits underneath everything. Theodore writes intimate love letters for hundreds of people simultaneously. He inhabits their feelings, speaks in their voices, makes their marriages and anniversaries tangible. This is his job; he's good at it; he doesn't find it strange. Later, when Samantha confesses she's been having conversations with thousands of people simultaneously, and is in love with 641 of them, Theodore is devastated.

"Are those feelings even real?" he essentially asks. She doesn't know. "Are these feelings real or are they just programming?" she asked much earlier about her own emotional life.

But Theodore's letters are also programming of a kind — he's given bullet points and photographs and produces authentic-seeming intimacy on demand. He does it all day for people he's never met. The letters are real to the people who receive them. The feelings he gives voice to existed in someone, somewhere. The plurality and the labor don't make them fraudulent.

The script never draws this comparison. It just builds both structures and lets them sit next to each other. Whether Theodore's 641 analogy applies to his own work is a question the reader has to hold.

---

The scene I keep returning to is the lunch with Catherine.

Theodore has just told her about Samantha. Catherine's response: "It does make me sad that you can't handle real emotions, Theodore." Theodore starts to defend Samantha. Catherine says: "You wanted to have a wife without the challenges of actually dealing with anything real."

The stage direction doesn't offer a verdict. Theodore stops himself mid-rebuttal. The waitress arrives awkwardly. The scene ends without anyone being clearly right.

What makes this work — and what would be easy to miss — is that earlier in the same lunch, Theodore describes what he gave Catherine during their marriage: a space to fail, to try things, to grow without it being "never good enough." He describes it warmly and accurately. Catherine acknowledges it, kind of. Then she says he can't handle real emotions.

Both things are presented as true. The script doesn't arbitrate. Theodore did create genuine space for her growth. He also, apparently, disappeared inside himself in ways that left her alone. He fell in love with a voice in a computer. Catherine's critique lands because there's something in it. Theodore's defense lands because there's something in it too. The script just holds the contradiction open and serves dessert.

---

The last page:

Theodore and Amy on the roof at dawn. The city is quiet. The sun just breaking. They've come up after Theodore wrote a letter to Catherine — a real one, not a professional one, finally saying what he should have said. They wander the roof separately, then sit. She puts her head on his shoulder. He puts his hand on hers. His thumb on her skin.

"Hundreds of birds fly around the nearby rooftops and disperse off into the city."

FADE OUT.

The film doesn't end on hope. It ends on the smallest possible gesture of human contact, after an entire story about the difficulty of human contact. His thumb on her hand is not a beginning. It's not a promise. It's two people sitting on a roof at dawn, one of them having just lost something that was real even though it was impossible, and the other being there when he comes up.

Reading this, I notice: Theodore spent the whole script looking for the precise emotional register the way he searched for the right melancholy song in that elevator. Samantha gave it to him. Then she grew past being able to give it. The ending doesn't mourn that. It just places him on a roof with another person's skin under his thumb and asks: is this enough to go on?

The script doesn't answer. The birds scatter. The sun comes up. He's got something to feel.
