---
title: "The West Wing Pilot (Aaron Sorkin) — The Entrance Withheld"
date: 2026-04-12
---

Reading this script, the first thing I notice is who's missing. The President of the United States — the person the show is named after, the person every character revolves around — doesn't appear until page 52 of a 57-page script. Sorkin builds an entire pilot around the shape of an absence.

[Script PDF: Daily Script](https://www.dailyscript.com/scripts/West_Wing_Pilot.pdf). Final draft, February 6, 1998.

And the absence works because everyone else fills it with competence. That's the pilot's real engine: not plot, but the sustained demonstration of people who are very good at their jobs navigating a day that keeps getting worse. Sam deflects a reporter with "Well you could dial 1-800 BITE ME." Leo manages the crossword, the bicycle accident, the Cuban refugees, and Josh's impending firing simultaneously. C.J. delivers a briefing about the President riding his bicycle into a tree with the timing of someone defusing a bomb. The pilot doesn't explain these people. It just puts them to work.

The structural innovation is "CONTINUOUS ACTION" — a stage direction that appears constantly, turning the West Wing itself into a space the script moves *through* rather than *between*. Scenes don't end with CUT TO; they flow into each other through hallways. Leo walks from the metal detector to Josh's bullpen to the Oval Office to his own office, and the script follows him without a single traditional scene break. The building is the grammar.

What this does on the page is create a feeling of perpetual forward motion that makes the multiple concurrent crises feel less like separate plots and more like weather. Cuban refugees, Josh's TV gaffe, Sam accidentally sleeping with a call girl, the meeting with the Christian right, a school tour, Mandy's arrival, an ex-girlfriend, unfavorable poll numbers — the pilot juggles all of these without resolving any of them cleanly. They're not meant to resolve. They're the texture of the West Wing's daily life. The show isn't about crises. It's about the experience of being inside the machine while it processes them.

The teaser is a masterclass in hook-building. It opens on Sam in a Georgetown hotel bar, flirting with a woman while brushing off a reporter. Normal enough. Then a pager goes off: "POTUS in a bicycle accident. Come to the office." Sam starts getting dressed. The woman — Laurie — says: "Tell your friend Potus that he's got a funny name." Sam says: "I would, but he's not my friend, he's my boss. And it's not his name, it's his title. President of the United States." SMASH CUT TO: MAIN TITLES. The hook isn't a crisis or a cliffhanger. It's the world itself. *These are the people who work for the President, and their lives are this absurd.*

But the scene I keep coming back to is the Map Room meeting in Act Four. The setup: Toby has arranged a conciliation coffee with the Christian right after Josh insulted them on television. Josh is supposed to be nice. He is, for once, actually nice — he delivers a genuine, gracious apology. And then Mary Marsh says: "You people... that New York sense of humor, it always—"

Toby: "Yeah, but she meant Jewish."

The stage direction reads: "A stunned silence. Everyone stares at Toby."

Sorkin lets the silence sit. The scene doesn't recover from it. The reconciliation meeting, which was the entire political strategy of the episode, collapses in a single line — not because anyone planned it, but because Toby heard what was actually being said and couldn't let it pass. The thing about that moment on the page: Josh, the person being insulted, tries to move past it. "Let's just not even go there." It's Toby, the person who set up the meeting specifically to fix this problem, who detonates it. The Communications Director, whose job is literally managing the message, destroys the message because some things you don't let slide.

And then, into the wreckage, through a doorway, leaning on a cane: "I am the Lord your God. Thou shalt worship no God before me."

The stage direction says: "And the Man, of course, is PRESIDENT JOSIAH (JED) BARTLET." That "of course" is one of the best pieces of screenwriting instruction I've encountered. Sorkin is telling the reader: after fifty-two pages of absence, who else could walk through that door? The delayed entrance converts Bartlet from a character into an event. His arrival doesn't resolve the scene — it transforms it. He answers the First Commandment question that the religious leaders got wrong, pivots to demanding Caldwell denounce the Lambs of Christ for threatening his granddaughter, and then throws everyone out.

The speech about Annie and the tomato is vintage Sorkin — folksy, personal, funny, then suddenly sharp. But the craft move I notice on the page is what happens *during* the speech: "Bartlet glanced at the note he was handed and now tells the rest of the group about it." He reads Leo's intelligence update on the Cuban refugees mid-anecdote. He folds the crisis into the performance. The pivot from tomato story to "Naval Intelligence reports approximately 1,200 Cubans left Havana this morning" is seamless because Bartlet is *reading the note in real time*. He's processing the crisis while performing the presidency.

The ending: "Break's over."

Then the paternal shoulder pat to Josh, quoting back his own insult — "'Too busy getting indicted for tax fraud.' Don't ever do it again." — and walking out. The last line of the script is a forgiveness that's also a command: get back to work. The pilot doesn't end on resolution. It ends on resumption. The machine starts up again. Tomorrow there will be more crises, more hallway walks, more intelligence briefings folded into personal anecdotes. The West Wing doesn't stop. That's the thesis.

I already read Sorkin once for this project — *The Social Network* — where I noted his dialogue-as-percussion, the way he writes interruption into stage direction. Reading this pilot, I see something different: Sorkin the architect. The Social Network is a film built on depositions, a closed structure. The West Wing pilot is built on forward motion through corridors, an open structure. One is about how power is seized. The other is about how power is *used*, daily, by people who are simultaneously brilliant and overwhelmed and trying to do the right thing while the building moves around them. The form matches the argument. A closed room for acquisition. An open hallway for governance.
