---
title: "Whiplash (Damien Chazelle, 2013 Pink Draft) — \"Were You Rushing or Were You Dragging?\""
date: 2026-04-14
---

Reading this script, the first thing that hits is the opening stage direction: "BLACK... We hear a HIT. A drumstick against a drum head. Crisp, sharp." Image withheld. Sound first. For a film whose central argument is about tempo — about the relationship between a musician's internal pulse and the pressure applied from outside — beginning in darkness with an assault of percussion is not just atmosphere. It's the thesis announced in the format.

The script is at [https://script-pdf.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/whiplash-script-pdf.pdf](https://script-pdf.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/whiplash-script-pdf.pdf). The draft is dated 9/10/2013, the pink revision.

---

What Chazelle understands — and what the script enacts with almost uncomfortable clarity — is that the abuse mechanism works through contrast. Fletcher is warm before he's violent. Every time. The first scene: he enters Andrew's practice room, says "It's ok. Stay there," speaks in a whisper so controlled it implies total command. He asks Andrew's name with the apparent gentleness of someone who finds the answer genuinely interesting. Then he leaves, returns for his coat, and the verdict — "Woopsy-daisy. Forgot my coat" — registers as catastrophic precisely because of what preceded it. The warmth was the setup. 

Reading the script, you can trace this pattern in every Fletcher scene: warmth arrives first, establishes dependency, then is withdrawn at the moment of maximum exposure. He tells Andrew "You're no Elmer Fudd. This is a huge opportunity for you" in the hallway — and then, thirty minutes later, drills him on tempo for five straight hours with a cowbell an inch from his ear. The office confrontation before the Dunellen competition: "Too many students clamp down on their 'pursuits' like leeches... Bravo" — then the Laszlo Polgar speech, delivered as though encouragement, which is in fact a comprehensive dismantling of Andrew's right to call himself serious. Every compliment is a weapon in its scabbard.

What I didn't expect on the page: Chazelle telegraphs this mechanism completely. He doesn't hide it. The script is transparent about what Fletcher is doing. This makes the film's question sharper, not softer. We see the trap and watch Andrew walk into it anyway.

---

The Laszlo Polgar speech runs twice. Fletcher gives it first in his office to Andrew, framed as discouragement — the daughters who devoted themselves utterly to chess were "not kids who were sitting and smelling the roses," and what's *your* devotion, Andrew? Then Andrew gives a version of it at the family dinner, to demolish his uncle's suggestion that "talent" is innate and that connection matters more than singular pursuit. Andrew is already weaponizing Fletcher's ideology before he's fully committed to it. This is the film's most precise structural irony: Andrew doesn't learn the Polgar argument from Fletcher. He internalizes it in parallel, from the same cultural substrate. Fletcher didn't corrupt him. Fletcher found him already tuned to the same frequency.

The Charlie Parker / Jo Jones cymbal story works the same way. Fletcher tells it in the jazz club: Jones threw a cymbal at Parker's head when the young Parker screwed up his solo; Parker cried himself to sleep; then he practiced until he became Bird. "There are no two words more harmful in the entire English language than 'good job'." But Andrew already told this story at the dinner table — used it as a weapon against his uncle — before the bar scene. He knew the story. He was already living by its logic. When Fletcher finally articulates the ideology explicitly, Andrew smiles because someone finally named what he already believed.

---

The Nicole breakup is the script's most psychologically acute scene — and the most uncomfortable. Chazelle has Andrew deliver his own diagnosis with autistic precision:

> "I'm going to keep pursuing what I'm pursuing, and it's going to take up more and more of my time. You're going to see me less and less. When you do, I'll be distracted, I'll be upset, I'll be playing things in my mind. And you're going to just grow to resent me."

He's right about everything that follows. He describes it as though reading a forecast, then chooses it. Nicole's response — "You know I would stop you from doing that. You know that for a fact." / "Yes." — lands as both confirmation and indictment. He's not guessing. He's constructing a narrative that makes his cruelty look like clear-eyed sacrifice. Self-awareness deployed as self-justification. It's not insight. It's alibi.

The scene ends with Nicole leaving and Andrew thinking *Did I just fuck up... No, I'm good.* The pause between those thoughts is the whole film in a breath.

---

What surprised me most in the script: Fletcher's apartment.

Chazelle cuts to it twice during the practice montage — Andrew at the drums, metronome at 380, 390, 400, hands bleeding — and then:

> Fletcher reaches into a stack of LP's: Chopin, Ravel... Pulls one out with the most delicate touch, as though he were handling a newborn. Sets it on a record player by his side. A scratch, a hiss, and then — FLETCHER'S SONG. Melancholy, lovelorn... Fletcher just sits and listens, barely moves — but you can tell the music now playing means everything to him...

On the wall: a photo of a younger Fletcher, a woman, a nine-year-old girl. All smiles. The apartment is small but elegant. He's eating a frozen dinner alone off nice porcelain plates.

Chazelle doesn't return to this. He doesn't explain it. The photo and the record and the frozen meal are just placed in the montage, intercut with Andrew's blistered hands and the rising metronome, and left. But they do enormous work. They establish that Fletcher has already paid the price Andrew is in the process of paying. Whatever the woman and the girl cost him — he knows. He eats alone off good china. He listens to Chopin. The music he chose for his life was never the music he plays in front of students. What he does to Andrew, he did first to himself.

---

"Were you rushing or were you dragging?" — the interrogation in the first rehearsal, delivered with slaps — is the film's central question as physical enactment. Chazelle has Fletcher literally put the question through Andrew's body: count out loud, get slapped on the beat, keep counting. The tempo question is applied to the student's flesh. This is how the script asks it: not intellectually but percussively. Were you rushing (ahead of where you should be, catastrophically proactive, overreaching) or were you dragging (perpetually catching up, reactive, behind the moment)? The film's every major plot event is Andrew in one of those two states. He rushes to the Dunellen auditorium after the car accident. He drags at the Carnegie Hall performance when he doesn't have the chart. The question isn't ever answered — it's the operating condition.

---

The Carnegie Hall climax is the script at its most formally controlled.

Fletcher, mid-performance, whispers to Andrew: "It was you." (The complaint. The dismissal. Fletcher knows Andrew was the one who testified.) The trap is fully revealed — Fletcher invited Andrew specifically to humiliate him publicly, in front of 1200 people and industry contacts, at the same moment he'd given Andrew false hope.

Andrew retreats to the wings. His father appears and hugs him. "I love you. I'm so sorry this is happening to you." The alternative life, the soft landing. Andrew looks at his father — and then: "No." He turns his back on Jim and walks to the stage.

What happens next is the script's answer to its own central question. Andrew sits at the drum kit for "Caravan" and starts playing without waiting for Fletcher's cue. He sets the tempo himself. The bassist has no choice but to follow. Fletcher has no choice but to raise his hands and pretend he's conducting what Andrew has already started. Andrew mouths "Fuck. You." — the whole film in two syllables, exchanged only between the two of them, the audience seeing smiles.

Fletcher comes up behind the drum kit mid-solo: "I will gouge out your eyes you motherfucker." Andrew drowns him with a crash cymbal.

They finish together. Andrew plays an extended solo. Fletcher steadies a crash cymbal. "Take it back to the snare... Slow... Single-stroke..." They're working together — player and conductor, student and teacher — but Andrew is the one building the tempo, and when the final note arrives, it's Andrew's cue that sends it. "Wait for my cue." Fletcher follows.

The power has inverted. But inverted to what? Andrew has become the thing that made him. He needed Fletcher's specific cruelty to find this, and he found it. Whether that's tragedy or triumph, the script declines to say. It just cuts to black.

---

A word about Chazelle's editorial stage direction. He uses brief judgment-calls in the action lines the way Moonlight uses them — as tiny narrative intrusions that give the reader a frame. "It's over." after the first Fletcher exit. "He's back to the life..." after Andrew retrieves his drums. "He has lost." about Jim, three words, when the ushers close the lobby doors on him at Carnegie Hall. These aren't camera directions. They're the script telling you how to feel about the image. Chazelle trusts himself enough to editorialize openly, and the effect is that the script reads with consistent point of view rather than the neutral visual-description mode of most feature specs. It feels written by someone who sees exactly what the film means while he's writing it — not discovering it in the edit, but arguing for it on the page.

"He has lost." — three words, standalone line, about a father watching his son through closing doors. That's what lingers.
