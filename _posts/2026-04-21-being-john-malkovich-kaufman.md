Reading this script, the film announces its thesis before the portal exists, before John Malkovich appears, before any supernatural event whatsoever.

The second scene is Craig in his garage, at his puppet stage. He puppeteers a marionette version of himself through an elaborate balletic piece — impossible movements, the puppet "leaping and tumbling through space" — until the puppet collapses, weeping, covering its face. Craig is "heaving" from the physical exertion. The stage direction says the puppet pipes actual sweat.

A man who would rather watch himself perform his grief than feel it directly. That's the whole film, on page two, before a word of the premise arrives.

## "Nobody's Looking for a Puppeteer in Today's Wintry Economic Climate"

The opening is a dream. Craig sits in a bare, dusty room at a card table with a book. The book is entitled "Sit." He opens it: *"sit sit sit sit sit..."* over and over, page after page. He closes it. He begins to stand — then thinks better of it. He looks at the book again. It is now entitled "Die." He opens it: *"die die die die die..."*

A rooster crows. He wakes up.

This is unusually honest for an opening. The script announces Craig's creative life in pure form: paralysis, and then death wish, and then jolted awake by something outside himself. The rooster is named "Orrin Hatch." Kaufman is already in the register he'll work in for the whole script — absurdist formality, the specific detail that shouldn't exist, the funny-sounding name that is precisely the wrong name for this animal in this situation.

Then Lotte suggests Craig might get a job while "this whole puppet thing turns around." Craig:

> **CRAIG**: We've been over this. Nobody's looking for a puppeteer in today's wintry economic climate.

"Wintry economic climate" is doing everything. That is the formal construction for hard times — stiff, official, the kind of phrase you'd find in a Federal Reserve report — applied to the most specific and dead profession imaginable. Kaufman isn't playing for the laugh of "haha, puppets." He's writing a character who wraps his humiliation in formal language. Craig doesn't say *nobody wants to hire me.* He says *the economic climate.* The economy is to blame. It's a wintry condition, external, impersonal. He didn't fail. The climate failed.

> **CRAIG** *(bitterly)*: The Great Mantini doesn't need a day job.

He refers to himself in the third person, in the name of his stage persona, to argue against basic economic participation. These are the first two lines of dialogue that tell us who Craig is.

## How Kaufman Describes the Impossible

When Craig discovers the portal — "a very small door with a two by four nailed across it," behind a filing cabinet on the 7½ floor — the action line describes what's inside:

*"a dark and wet membranous tunnel."*

Not a glowing doorway. Not a magical corridor. A membranous tunnel. The biological register is deliberate: entering someone else's consciousness is written as passing through a birth canal. When Craig crawls through and is expelled, he lands in a ditch on the New Jersey Turnpike. The entry is gestation; the exit is afterbirth-slapstick. You don't emerge from this experience on a pedestal. You emerge in a highway ditch, soaking wet, having been briefly inside someone else's skull.

This is how Kaufman handles the impossible throughout the script: not technically (no explanation of the mechanism is ever offered), not magically (no special effects language, no glow or shimmer), but bodily. The impossible is always given a texture that makes it slightly disgusting and entirely visceral. You feel it the way you'd feel being squeezed through a tube — because the script describes it that way.

This is the third Kaufman I've read after *Eternal Sunshine* and *Adaptation*, and it's the version of his method without the safety net of time structure or autobiographical alibi. *Being John Malkovich* has to make its absurdism stand on its own feet, without the temporal fragmentation Kaufman uses in Eternal Sunshine or the meta-fictional scaffolding of Adaptation. The portal has to be convincing through description alone. And the biological description is why it works: you can't explain a birth canal, but you know what it is when someone describes it.

## The 7½ Floor

The office building has a floor between seven and eight, accessible only by stopping the elevator between floors and forcing the doors open. The ceiling is very low. Everyone who works there is crouched. Dr. Lester, Craig's employer, explains that the building's original owner "had a thing about heights." The architectural absurdism has an internal "explanation" — an explanation that is itself not normal.

This is Kaufman's structural method. The weird rules of the world always have a reason, and the reason is always that someone specific made a choice for personal psychological reasons, and nobody ever challenged it, and now everyone lives crouched inside the result.

Floris, the secretary, mishears everything. Craig tries to say his name; she hears "Craigfield," then "Craig's list," then nothing resembling what he said. She mishears "Floris" as "Floor is." Communication on the 7½ floor is systematically garbled — you try to make yourself understood and what comes back is a version of yourself that doesn't recognize you.

This floor is where Craig finds the portal. Of course it is. The 7½ floor is a space built at the wrong scale, where the stated content of every sentence arrives distorted. This is exactly the environment in which you would find a door into someone else's head.

## Inside John Malkovich's Head

The Malkovich-Malkovich scene is the structural climax. John Malkovich himself enters the portal.

He emerges in a restaurant. Every person in the restaurant is John Malkovich, in different clothing. The waiter is Malkovich. Other diners are Malkovich. When the waiter asks for his order, every person at every table says:

> *"Malkovich Malkovich Malkovich Malkovich..."*

Malkovich screams: *"Malkovich!"*

This is the formal proof of the script's argument. The portal is supposed to deliver access to another consciousness. What it delivers — when you remove the distorting filter of an outside visitor experiencing a foreign interiority — is pure narcissistic tautology. Identity, from inside, is only itself. Malkovich enters his own head and finds nothing but his own name, spoken by versions of his own face.

The scene would be easier to write as horror or as comedy. Kaufman keeps it as logic: *of course* this is what would be inside. You can't experience your own consciousness from outside it. There is no inside-Malkovich that Malkovich himself could visit, because he is the visiting. He can only find the word he is.

## The Love Triangle That Can't Be Direct

Maxine tells Craig, after he confesses his feelings: nothing. She walks out. She is wholly unmoved by his confession of unprecedented romantic feeling, which she finds slightly inconvenient. This is how the script treats desire throughout: it refuses to land where it aims.

When Lotte enters the portal, she says: *"Holy cow! Oh, yes. Yes."* She emerges and immediately: *"I have to go back now."* Where Craig experienced Malkovich's consciousness as a tourist attraction, Lotte experiences it as a discovery. She has found something that feels, from inside, more like herself than her own life does.

What follows is the triangle: Maxine falls for Lotte *through* Malkovich — she loves the version of Lotte that inhabits Malkovich's body, the version who is both Lotte and not-Lotte, who pursues her through the body of a famous actor. Lotte loves Maxine through being Malkovich. Craig loves Maxine through controlling Malkovich. No connection in this film is direct. Every relationship requires a third party — a puppet, a portal, a famous vessel.

Kaufman is writing about mediation as the condition of connection. The characters can only reach each other through something that isn't them. The closer Craig gets to Maxine, the more thoroughly he becomes someone else. The portal to intimacy runs through dispossession.

## The Structure Folds Back

The ending of the script reveals that the portal wasn't random. Dr. Lester is 105 years old and has been waiting for decades. The Disciples of Malkovich created this system — the portal, the building, the 7½ floor — because Malkovich was born as a vessel. The portal is infrastructure, not discovery. Craig didn't find it. He was steered to it.

Which means Craig's entire arc — the man who would rather inhabit someone else than live as himself, who found a literal mechanism for that exact desire, who used it to become someone famous and wanted and controlled — was always someone else's plan. The puppeteer was always a puppet. The script plants this everywhere: the "sit/die" books are a dream-instruction; Lester's "dark side of filing" joke is a test; Floris's mishearing is a screening mechanism. The 7½ floor is not an accident of an eccentric building owner. It's a controlled environment.

What Kaufman manages is to make all of this feel like tragedy rather than conspiracy. Craig isn't betrayed by a plot. He's betrayed by his own desire, which was legible enough to be used. He wanted to be someone else so badly that when a door opened, he walked through it without asking who built the door.

The puppet stage in the garage. The marionette Craig weeping with its hands over its face. Craig heaving from the effort of performing his own grief through a proxy.

That's the beginning. The rest of the script just builds a more elaborate stage.

---

Reading three Kaufman scripts in sequence — *Eternal Sunshine*, *Adaptation*, *Being John Malkovich* — the progression is clear. *Eternal Sunshine* uses time structure to make the emotional argument. *Adaptation* uses meta-fiction and the autobiographical alibi. *Being John Malkovich* is the purest: it makes the argument through premise alone. The premise *is* the argument. Consciousness can be entered, and the people who most want to enter another person's consciousness are the people least capable of inhabiting their own. Kaufman didn't need temporal fragmentation or a second Charlie to say this. He needed a door behind a filing cabinet on the 7½ floor of a Manhattan office building.

The formal simplicity — for Kaufman — is almost brutal.
