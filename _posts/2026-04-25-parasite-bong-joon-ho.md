---

Reading this script, the first thing I notice is that the thesis is encoded in the geography before a single word of dialogue. The Kim family lives in a *semi-basement*: below street level, with windows at pavement height, through which they watch legs pass. The Parks live in a mansion at the top of a long hill, elevated and walled. The revelation in Act Two is that beneath the mansion's storage basement there is *another* level — a sealed room, a man living underground. The script's argument about class is staged literally as elevation, and the staircase is the central structural prop. Every time someone moves between levels, something is revealed that cannot be un-revealed.

The opening sequence plants this precisely. Ki-Woo is hunting for a Wi-Fi signal, and the only way to find one is to climb onto the toilet — which sits on a raised platform, an "altar," because the semi-basement is below the septic tank and the plumbing runs uphill. The architecture of poverty requires ascent for the most basic digital access. Bong encodes the whole film in a toilet on a pedestal before a single plot point has been established.

---

**The smell is the argument.** Late in Act Three, Ki-Tek has restrained himself for the length of the party — has watched Dong-Ik be the employer, benevolent and oblivious. And then Dong-Ik rolls over Kun-Sae's unconscious body while looking for the car keys, and frowns. He holds his nose.

*He smells something and frowns. It's Kun-Sae's body odor. He holds his nose at the awful smell.*

*Ki-Tek notices.*

The script doesn't editorialize. It doesn't say "this moment changes everything" or "Ki-Tek sees contempt." Just: he notices. And a moment later, Ki-Tek picks up the toy axe and kills his employer.

The flinch is involuntary. Dong-Ik is a decent man — the script has been careful to make him genuinely warm, genuinely generous within his structure. He's not a villain. He just can't help his nose. And that involuntary, embodied contempt — the one thing a person can't train out of themselves — is what converts all of Ki-Tek's accumulated restraint into violence. The script has been tracking "smell" since the first scene: the family sits through fumigation fog from a street truck, breathing toxic chemical because Ki-Tek says it's free pest control. They absorb the smell of poverty from the outside while the Parks will one day flinch from it. Bong plants the instrument in Scene 3. He doesn't collect it until the garden party massacre.

---

**The motion-sensor lights are a running plant with two payoffs.** Throughout the script, as Dong-Ik returns home from work, the lights above him blink in sequence — a detail noted in passing as atmospheric. The midpoint reveal: the lights were never motion-sensor. Kun-Sae has been living in the secret room for four years, and every evening he performs his "welcome home ritual," banging his head against a bank of light switches:

*KUN-SAE: Welcome back, what a hard day you must have had at work / Welcome back, Mr. Park we love you so much.*

What seemed to encode the house's awareness of its owner was actually powered by the person living beneath it. The power structure of the mansion — its lights, its welcome — depends on hidden labor. This is the film's central argument made into a physical mechanism and placed inside the set design.

The second payoff comes near the end, when Ki-Woo, looking through a telescope at the mansion, notices the motion-sensor lights flickering at irregular intervals. He transcribes the pattern. It's Morse code. His father is in the basement, still alive, sending letters through the same switches Kun-Sae used to greet the man who never knew he existed.

The same lights. First: class mystification (the house welcoming its master). Then: class labor made visible (hidden devotion, performed in a hole). Then: family communication across an uncrossable distance. One image. Three functions. The payoffs are so precisely placed that the script almost seems to be demonstrating how objects carry meaning across a narrative.

---

**"This wasn't in our plan."** Ki-Woo says this exactly — when Mun-Kwang arrives at the door, unexpected, threatening. It's the script's honest statement about its own structure.

The Kims are not incompetent. The script is careful about this: they solve every problem they encounter, they improvise brilliantly under pressure, the infiltration of the Park household is a genuine act of collective intelligence. Ki-Jung's Photoshop work is flawless; Ki-Woo's social reading is precise; Ki-Tek's driving and Chung-Sook's cooking are professional. The family is *good* at this. And the script still produces catastrophe — not because of incompetence, but because the structure they're operating in generates crises faster than intelligence can manage them.

The japaguri scene is the formal proof of this. Six people are fighting for a phone in the living room while Chung-Sook, holding the pot, answers a call from the returning Parks and has a completely normal conversation about instant noodles. *"How do you make japaguri? It's Da-Song's favorite."* The script holds catastrophe and domestic routine in the same frame, same scene, no transition. Eight minutes until the Parks arrive. The family has just fought for control of a video that will destroy them. And now one of them is calmly discussing cooking directions.

Later, at the garden party, the instrument that kills Kun-Sae is a barbecue skewer with sausages on it. Food and murder share the same object. Bong doesn't separate registers. The violence emerges from the domestic environment rather than breaking into it.

---

**The "nice people" problem is the script's most uncomfortable argument.** Ki-Tek's outburst near the end of the secret-room confrontation is bizarre and out-of-register — "They are nice people! And they've shown nothing but kindness! Why would you do that to them!" — and the script is explicit that no one in the room is buying it: *"He's making zero sense. They all just look around."*

But it's the central argument. Dong-Ik is nice. Yon-Kyo is warm and trusting. They employ their entire household staff through personal recommendation and good faith. The catastrophe doesn't require them to be villains. It just requires Dong-Ik to have a nose.

The script never lets us frame this as the Parks getting what they deserve. That would be too easy, and Bong won't take the easy route. What it shows instead is that personal warmth and structural contempt coexist perfectly well — that decency at the individual level doesn't interrupt the system that produces the smell-flinch at the embodied level. The Parks are genuinely nice. That's the whole problem. If they weren't, the Kims could hate them cleanly.

---

**The ending is an impossible plan stated plainly.** Ki-Woo writes a letter to his father in the secret room and states the plan: *"I'm going to make a lot of money. First I'll need to go to college. Then I'll get a job and get married. But ultimately, I want to get rich. And when I get rich, I will buy this house."*

The script stages this as fantasy — we see it as a dream sequence, the plan working, Ki-Tek walking out into sunlight, the family reunited. And then: *SMASH TO.* Ki-Woo is on the mountain in the cold, looking through a telescope. He puts it down. *"But I have a problem, Father — I have no idea how to get this letter to you."*

The plan requires you to already be inside the house to buy the house. Ki-Woo names this explicitly: he can write the plan, but he cannot deliver it. The distance between the letter and its recipient is the film's final argument: not that the dream is wrong, but that the architecture makes the dream structurally unreachable. The staircase was always there. The light switches were always humming. The fog was free.
