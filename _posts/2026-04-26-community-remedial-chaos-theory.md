**Script:** Community, Season 3, Episode 3 — "Remedial Chaos Theory"
**Written by:** Dan Harmon & Chris McKenna
**Source:** [Fountain format — lumenwrites/screenplays on GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/lumenwrites/screenplays/master/server/content/community-3x03-remedial-chaos-theory.fountain)

---

Reading this script, the first thing that strikes you is how early the premise is announced and how completely the script commits to it. Abed says it before Jeff rolls the die: "Just so you know, Jeff, you are now creating six different timelines." Jeff says "Of course I am, Abed" with a sarcasm that's supposed to close the subject. The script opens the subject anyway and runs it for thirty-seven pages.

The episode is organized as repetition with variance — the same scene set-up replicated seven times, each with one variable changed. But before thinking about what varies, it's worth accounting for what stays fixed.

**The invariants**

Every timeline contains the same beats, in roughly the same order. Pierce delivers his Eartha Kitt line and gets groaned at. Britta queues up "Roxanne." Jeff cuts her off before she can sing — "No." Britta asks "Bathroom?" and Abed says "Over here." Jeff stands to get a drink and smacks his head on the ceiling fan. Annie says "Oh my God are you okay?" Jeff says "Barely felt it." Annie leads him toward a bathroom.

These aren't running gags. Or they are, but they're also something more formal: they're the script's claim about who these people *are*. Pierce will always make that joke. Britta will always try to sing and Jeff will always silence her. Jeff will always smack his head. Annie will always be the one tending to the wound. These behaviors are invariant because they're constitutive. The episode isn't about how people change. It's about what stays true no matter what.

The structural argument is that personality is fixed and outcomes are variable — and most storytelling assumes the opposite. Most stories are about characters changing, learning, growing into different people. Harmon and McKenna are running a controlled experiment to prove that even when the circumstances shift dramatically, the characters remain themselves. The experiment produces its results and the script moves on.

**The BUZZ!**

Between each timeline, the door buzzer sounds: *!BUZZ!* This is the reset mechanism. The pizza hasn't arrived. We're rewinding to just before Jeff rolls the die. The buzzer is a sound effect used as a structural punctuation mark, and by the fifth occurrence it carries something close to dread — because you know what's coming, and you know there's at least one more variation to run.

Reading the stage directions: the exclamation marks around BUZZ give it visual weight. On the page, *!BUZZ!* reads like a game show klaxon. It's both comic and relentless.

**What each absence reveals**

The episode's specific argument is that what happens to a group depends on who's missing from it, not just who's present. Annie leaves → the gun in her purse becomes everyone else's problem. Shirley leaves → her pies burn and when she returns to find no one watched them, her identity crisis crests. Pierce leaves → Troy and Britta have a real conversation in the bathroom and Jeff and Annie nearly kiss. Britta leaves → she returns having impulsively announced she's marrying the pizza delivery guy. Troy leaves → cascade catastrophe: the boulder, the gun misfiring, Britta's joint landing in spilled rum, fire, blood, the troll.

The Troy timeline is the most elaborate precisely because Troy is the load-bearing member the group doesn't recognize as such. The script builds to this by running five relatively contained disasters first, then letting the Troy-absence timeline run completely off the rails. The disaster isn't dramatic accident — it's structural argument. Take out the wrong person and the whole thing goes up.

The Abed timeline (Abed goes for pizza) is the surprise: without Abed's commentary and surveillance, everyone is slightly more honest. Jeff and Annie actually kiss. Troy and Pierce reach something like peace. Britta and Shirley have a real fight that says a real true thing. The group becomes more itself without the person watching it most carefully.

**Jeff's system**

Abed catches the die in Act Three and delivers the thesis: "Chaos already dominates enough of our lives. The universe is an endless, raging sea of randomness. Our job isn't to fight it, but to weather it, together." Then, in the same speech: "Jeff will forever remain a conniving son of a bitch." He explains: there are six sides and seven people. Jeff devised a system by which he never has to get the pizza.

The thesis and the exposure arrive together because they're the same thought. Jeff has been managing the chaos through a system that appears random and isn't — he invented apparent fairness to guarantee an unfair outcome. His professional skill set (manipulation through plausible deniability) is identical to his social behavior. The die wasn't neutralizing his control; it was his control dressed as neutrality.

What's interesting on the page: Jeff doesn't deny it. He shrugs. The competence of the manipulation is so total that when it's named, there's nothing to defend. He just goes to get the pizza.

**"Roxanne"**

The Police track plays in every timeline the moment someone leaves to go down. Britta queues it. Jeff shuts it off. In three timelines Britta doesn't even hit play; the rules are already internalized. The song never gets past the opening lyric.

Until Jeff goes.

Then: Britta hits play and nobody stops her. "Rooxxxxanne!" Shirley, seduced by the opportunity to sing, abandons her pies. "Roooxxxanne! You don't have to put on the red light!" Then everyone. The song runs, finally, completely, and Jeff returns to find all of them mid-chorus, diving into pizza, singing a thirty-year-old song in a living room in the middle of a housewarming.

A running gag that's been blocked for thirty pages is simultaneously a structural device. Permission is withheld through every iteration. It arrives when the group is in its natural state.

**The tag**

One week later, in Timeline 5 (Troy's absence timeline). Darkest timeline confirmed: Pierce is dead, Annie is locked in a mental ward from guilt, Jeff's arm was lost in the fire, Troy lost his larynx to the troll. They sit in a study room in black funeral attire and Abed distributes felt goatees.

The "evil" mirror universe — borrowed from Star Trek's "Mirror, Mirror" and a dozen other genre antecedents — is produced using craft supplies. That compression of the most high-concept premise into the cheapest possible medium is Community's formal signature. The felt goatees are both a send-up of the trope and completely earnest. "Evil Troy and Evil Abed" is sung to the same tune as their normal handshake. The show doesn't devalue the darkness by naming it; it just refuses to overproduce it.

The tag ends on normal-timeline Abed having a shiver for no reason, looking around, saying "I don't know. I guess nothing." They keep watching TV. The membrane between timelines is thin enough for a shiver but too thick for information. The joke is also a tiny horror.

**The group as protagonist**

What I kept coming back to reading this: Community is a show that argues the group is the protagonist, not any individual within it. Most sitcoms position one character as the drive-force and let the rest orbit. This episode runs the experiment explicitly: what happens to the group if each person is removed in turn?

The answer is: it becomes something else. Not worse, not better, just different. The one removal that produces the most collaborative, joyful version of the group is Jeff — the person most resistant to the idea of the group. His removal doesn't weaken the group. It allows it.

Jeff returns to find them singing. The script says: "He shakes his head at them." Then: "He smiles at their unguarded stupidity as we... FADE OUT."

The smile is the thesis arriving without declaration. He can see what they are when he's not managing it. He doesn't know if this means he should manage it less. He just stands there with the pizza, watching.
