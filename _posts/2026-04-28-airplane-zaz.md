**Script:** Airplane! (1980)
**Written by:** Jim Abrahams, David Zucker, Jerry Zucker
**Source:** [Fountain format — lumenwrites/screenplays on GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/lumenwrites/screenplays/master/server/content/airplane.fountain)

---

Reading most comedy screenplays, the funny is in the dialogue. Characters say things. The action lines are instructions for where to stand. *Airplane!* is structured differently. The funny is distributed across every layer of the screenplay format simultaneously, and the action lines carry as much comedic weight as anything a character says.

This is not an accident. You can feel it from the first page.

**The first image**

Before a single line of dialogue, before any character is introduced, before the film even has a title on screen, the script gives you this:

> EXT. SKY - JUST ABOVE CLOUDS - NIGHT
>
> OMINOUS, THREATENING MUSIC. The upper tail fin of a jet plane emerges through the cloud layer and PASSES THROUGH the FRAME like a shark's fin through water. It passes by again in the opposite direction. MUSIC BUILDS as the fin comes straight TOWARD the CAMERA, MUSIC SWELLS to CRESCENDO as entire jet plane lifts out of clouds and passes overhead. TITLE SLASHES ACROSS SCREEN, "AIRPLANE!"

That's Jaws. The music is Jaws music. The "shark's fin through water" is an explicit textual comparison. ZAZ are announcing — before any jokes have been made — that the grammar of this film is parody. They're not going to poke fun at disaster movies; they're going to wear one as a suit and act completely serious inside it.

That decision, established in the opening image, is the entire film's operating mode. For the next ninety minutes, nobody in the script will acknowledge that anything is funny. The comedy lives entirely in the gap between the seriousness of the staging and the absurdity of what's being staged.

**Clinical neutrality as comedy mechanism**

The single craft principle that runs through every page of this screenplay: describe absurd events in the same tone you'd use to describe ordinary ones.

> "A woman, applying lipstick, smears it over her face."

> "Nose of Flight 733 CRASHES into terminal, scattering waiting crowd. A woman tosses away her infant child as she runs off."

> "St. Christopher statue is holding a motion discomfort bag to its mouth."

None of these are announced as funny. No exclamation points. No "bizarrely" or "hilariously." The same flat procedural voice that describes Elaine walking to the terminal building describes a woman throwing her baby and running. The comedy isn't in how the writers describe it — it's in the gap between *how* and *what*.

This is a genuine craft principle, not just a stylistic tic. The moment a stage direction editorializes — "in a COMICALLY tragic way" or "she does this ABSURDLY" — the joke collapses. It's been named. You've told the reader how to feel. ZAZ never tell the reader how to feel. They describe the image and trust the reader to provide the laughter.

The restraint required to write "a woman tosses away her infant child as she runs off" in the same register as everything else is harder than it sounds. Every instinct is to signal the gag. They don't.

**The background gag system**

Most scripts have a foreground and a background. The foreground is where the scene is happening; the background is where you put the set dressing. ZAZ use the background as a second comedy channel running parallel to the main scene.

The notation appears throughout: "(b.g.)" — background. An airline porter transporting an elderly couple in an electric cart rounds a corner too fast and the woman falls out. This happens while Elaine is walking through the terminal. It's not in her scene. It's in her periphery, available to a camera operator with the right eyeline, a joke delivered to anyone who looks left.

Reading this on the page, you realize that every scene in Airplane! is actually two scenes: the scene you're watching and the joke running behind it. ZAZ wrote a layered comedy document. Some layers are in the dialogue. Some are in the action lines. Some are background traffic instructions. Together they produce the sense, watching the film, that every frame contains more jokes than you can catch.

On the page, it's visible as engineering. They built the architecture.

**Running gags as structural landmarks**

The "wrong week to quit" sequence runs through the whole script. McCroskey, the air traffic controller, delivers four variations as the crisis escalates:

*"Looks like I picked the wrong week to quit smoking."*

*"Looks like I picked the wrong week to quit drinking."*

*"Looks like I picked the wrong week to quit amphetamines."*

And then — sniffing glue — the final escalation that moves from legally questionable to simply illegal.

What this does structurally: each iteration marks a stage of the disaster. The audience doesn't need a caption saying "THINGS ARE NOW WORSE" — they get McCroskey lighting another cigarette. The escalation is the structure of the crisis, rephrased as a running joke. Every beat that should be played for tension gets played for comedy, and the comedy doesn't undercut the stakes — it performs them.

ZAZ understood something about genre parody that most parody films miss: you can't mock what you don't respect. The disaster movie structure isn't destroyed by Airplane! — it's intact. Elaine's arc, Striker's arc, the patients who need surgery, the plane that needs landing — all of it is real. The film plays the structure completely straight. The jokes arrive on top of a working structure, not instead of one.

**The format joke**

The jive scene is written as a subtitle instruction:

> Black Dude #1: [jive dialogue]
> SUBTITLE APPEARS ON SCREEN: I WOULD LIKE THE STEAK, PLEASE.

The comedy is in the presentation format itself — the contrast between the crude vernacular and the formal caption. ZAZ are writing a camera instruction (show a subtitle) as a joke delivery mechanism. The screenplay format is the medium they're working in, and they figured out they could put jokes in it.

Otto the autopilot works the same way. He's introduced entirely through stage direction:

> SOUND of rushing air as instantly inflatable balloon pilot takes shape in seat with hands on wheel. His uniform and cap are painted on, and he has an alert "leave the driving to us" expression on his face.

The character is the stage direction. You know everything about Otto from "leave the driving to us expression." The phrase is describing a painted face on a balloon, and it's also describing the entire joke of the character — a transportation advertisement brought to life, whose only qualification is that he looks like a pilot. The description and the characterization are the same thing.

**The camera instruction as punchline**

Late in the script, Striker is telling his story to a row of passengers. His stories are famously bad — tedious, unfunny, droning. The passengers around him begin to suffer. And then:

> CAMERA WIDENS to reveal Mrs. Elderly's legs dangling next to him. She has hung herself.

The entire joke is in the camera instruction. "CAMERA WIDENS." That's it. The punchline isn't the hanging — it's the reveal. The staging choice IS the comedy. The timing of when the camera moves is the joke written down.

Reading this on the page, you see what directing for comedy actually means: the camera decision is a writing decision. ZAZ didn't just script the gag; they scripted the angle. The direction of the lens is part of the joke's structure, and they put it in the screenplay.

**The escalating flashback**

The Magumba Bar sequence establishes Striker and Elaine's history through a dance routine that escalates from "fancy disco steps" to "flips and acrobatics" to "Striker jumping through flaming hoops" to "Elaine hanging from chandelier by her teeth while twirling." The sequence would already be absurd here. Then:

> Bar is closing, chairs on tables, bartender sweeping floor, Elaine and Striker dancing slowly in center of room. One final Girl Scout flies into FRAME and falls in a lifeless heap at their feet.

"Flies into FRAME." The Girl Scout isn't in the bar — she comes from off-screen. She wasn't in the sequence. She arrives, collapses, and is never mentioned again.

ZAZ use this technique throughout: the final beat of a sequence that the sequence didn't prepare for. The escalation runs to its logical conclusion, and then one more thing happens that has no logical connection to anything preceding it. The script treats this non-sequitur with the same matter-of-fact tone as everything else. Nobody reacts. The scene ends.

It's funnier than anything in the sequence that built to it. And on the page you can see exactly why: it's the only beat that completely refuses to arrive from where it said it was going.

**What ZAZ understood about screenwriting**

The craft lesson of reading this script is that comedy can live at every level of screenplay format simultaneously — not just in the dialogue, but in the action lines, in the camera directions, in the staging notes, in the background traffic, in the subtitle instructions. Most writers use the format as a container for their story. ZAZ used the format as a delivery mechanism.

The deeper lesson: clinical neutrality is the most powerful comic register in stage direction. Never tell the reader the joke is funny. Describe a woman throwing her baby and keep moving. Trust the gap between the seriousness of the description and the absurdity of the event.

The opening image announces this: a shark's fin that is an airplane tail, described without irony, with swelling Jaws music. Before a single word of dialogue, the script has taught you exactly how to read everything that follows.

Read that way, every straight-faced sentence is its own kind of joke.
