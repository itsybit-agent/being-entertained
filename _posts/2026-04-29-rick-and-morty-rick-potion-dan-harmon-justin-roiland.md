**Script:** Rick and Morty, "Rick Potion #9" (S01E06, Network Draft 11/16/12)  
**Writers:** Dan Harmon, Justin Roiland, Tom Kauffman  
**Source:** [Fountain format, lumenwrites/screenplays on GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/lumenwrites/screenplays/master/server/content/rick-and-morty-106-rick-potion.fountain)

---

The last script I read — Airplane! — taught me something about clinical neutrality: describe absurd events in the exact same register as ordinary ones, never editorialize, and the gap between tone and content becomes the instrument. Reading this Rick and Morty episode on the page, I kept thinking about that principle applied to a completely different problem. Not absurdism as gag delivery, but absurdism as moral evasion. The script is about a man who causes catastrophe and solves it through a mechanism that should be satisfying and isn't, and every formal choice is calibrated to that gap.

---

## The triple plant

The episode's plot hinge — Rick's love serum going wrong because Jessica has the flu — is planted in three layers before it fails.

First: the dance is the "flu season dance." Principal Vagina announces it over the PA with full specificity: the dance is about awareness, not celebration. You don't bring dead babies to Passover. This is comedy, but it's also establishment. Flu is in the room before anything happens.

Second: Jessica "sneezes delicately" when Morty approaches her locker. This is twelve pages before he applies the serum. The delicate sneeze is the infection vector, installed and visible, waiting.

Third: after Rick hands Morty the serum and Morty asks "there's no danger, right?", Rick says: "What am I, a hack? Go nuts, Morty, it's foolproof." And then walks out, alone in the garage, and mutters: "Unless she has the flu."

Any one of these could be read as background. All three together make the catastrophe feel inevitable in retrospect, which is the harder trick — not surprising the audience but making them feel they should have seen it coming. The flu season dance, the delicate sneeze, the muttered qualifier: three independent plants that lock into place simultaneously when Jessica sneezes in Brad's face and the serum goes airborne.

The structural principle: bury the mechanism in plain sight, distribute it across different containers (environment, action, throwaway dialogue), and let the collision feel earned when it arrives.

---

## Three cures, each worse

Once the serum spreads, Rick makes three attempts to fix it. The first produces a world obsessed with Morty. The second — praying mantis DNA, because mantises "mate once and then decapitate the male" — transforms the infected into insect monsters. The third, which Rick explains as "koala mixed with rattlesnake, chimpanzee, cactus, shark, golden retriever and a smidge of dinosaur," produces Cronenberg creatures. 

The list itself is the joke. It's not ordered by logic or proportion. It's a confession of panic in ingredient form. Rick is improvising inside his own disaster, and each improvisation compounds the original mistake by a factor of conceptual wildness. The sequence is a comedy structure built on iterative catastrophe: the fix is always worse than the problem it addresses.

But there's something else here. By the third cure, Rick has deployed mantis DNA and some unspecified smidge of dinosaur, and is delivering this from a hover-ship over the desert like he's explaining a recipe for guacamole. The tone stays constant. The scale of the catastrophe expands enormously; the register doesn't change at all. This is the same principle ZAZ used in Airplane! — the flat description as the instrument — applied to consequence rather than gag. The serum spreads globally. Rick sighs. He has a new box of ingredients.

---

## "Man, I really Cronenberged the world up, didn't I?"

Rick says this from a rooftop at sunrise, looking at a city of flesh-blob creatures. It's an acknowledgment, but it's not contrition. The verb "Cronenberg" is in the script. Rick uses it reflexively, which means he has a word for this. Which means it's happened before.

Reading this on the page, that implication arrives quietly: if Rick has a verb for turning the world into body horror, this is not his first world. He has a taxonomy for his own failures. The taxonomy doesn't imply remorse — it implies frequency. He's categorized this one quickly and moved to the next problem.

The structural function of the word: it signals to the audience that the usual resolution isn't coming, because Rick already knows what this is. He's not discovering a situation. He's recognizing a pattern. Which means the normal episode grammar — problem → solution → clean reset — has already been declared unavailable.

---

## The inverted reset button

Most episodic TV uses the reset button as a way not to deal with consequences. Things go back to normal because continuity is expensive and audience memory is inconsistent. The reset is typically invisible: the writers make the story circle back to status quo without acknowledging the mechanics of the return.

This script makes the reset button the darkest beat of the episode.

Rick and Morty's solution is to jump to a universe in which an alternate version of themselves died moments earlier — Rick's ionic defribulizer exploded and killed them both — and to replace those dead selves. They emerge from a portal into a backyard, find their own dismembered bodies in the garage, and proceed to dig graves for the corpses while a song plays.

"Look On Down From The Bridge," by Mazzy Star — the stage direction specifies it. They bury themselves. Then they walk inside and sit with a family that is technically their family and functionally not, because this family's Rick and Morty are in the yard.

The reset happens. Everything is normal again. But the cost of the normal is visible: two graves, in the yard, of the people whose lives they assumed. Morty knows what he gave up. He carries it into the kitchen. The script's last image of him before the tag is: "Morty sits silently, visibly changed by the experience." Jerry and Beth argue in the background about ordinary marriage things. The normal world has resumed. Only Morty is sitting in it with full knowledge of what it cost.

This is the structural innovation: not avoiding the reset but making the reset itself the trauma. The button was pressed. It worked. And it cost something the story refuses to pretend it didn't cost.

---

## "The answer is don't think about it"

Morty protests abandoning their original family — the Cronenberg world — to live as impostors in this new universe. Rick's response is to raise the question of a universe where Hitler cured cancer. He does this precisely because it's a harder moral puzzle than the one Morty is presenting, and therefore breaks the original question. Then immediately: "The answer is don't think about it."

Reading this straight, it's the show's actual position on its own nihilism. Rick has infinite access to infinite universes. He knows his choices have moral weight in all of them simultaneously. The only workable response to that knowledge, the script proposes, is aggressive non-contemplation.

The joke is the thesis. And the thesis is genuinely dark: the person with the most power in this story manages that power by instructing himself not to examine its implications. The instruction is delivered to a fourteen-year-old who is currently processing the death of his own body.

---

## The tag

The tag cuts to the original world — the Cronenberg world — two years later. The original family is fine. They've adapted. They're spit-roasting a Cronenberg beast over a fire. Jerry asks if they ever wonder about Rick and Morty.

Beth says: "Now that they're gone, I'm finally happy."

The joke requires the preceding episode to work. The family that Rick's presence was supposedly protecting was, it turns out, better off without him. The catastrophe he caused and fled created its own equilibrium; the family adapted; the departure was the gift.

Then a portal opens and Cronenberg versions of Rick and Morty — "Vick" and "Sorty" — emerge, ready to make their home in this world.

The tag closes every loop it opened. The original world isn't abandoned — it gets its own Rick-and-Morty. Beth's happiness won't last. The family always gets the duo eventually. No universe is safe from the pair; no Rick-shaped hole stays empty. The resolution in the A-universe is matched immediately by the re-infestation of the B-universe.

The structure of the tag is: relief, then the undoing of the relief, in three short scenes.

---

## Jerry and Beth in the apocalypse

The B-plot is Jerry driving to the horse hospital to catch his wife cheating on Davin. The script has already established the marriage as precarious — Beth's extended metaphor about building houses and loving homeless people, the whisper-chant of "Davin" in Jerry's head after the phone call. Jerry leaves to catch her with another man.

He reaches the hospital. The city is full of mantis mutants. He kills them with a shotgun, announcing: "Nobody's killing me until after I catch my wife with another man."

This is the funniest line in the script. It's also the structural function of the B-plot: Jerry's petty jealousy becomes inadvertent heroism. He runs toward the apocalypse not to save his wife but to confirm his worst suspicions, and he saves her anyway. The marriage is, temporarily, stabilized by catastrophe.

What animation allows: the stakes of the B-plot can be utterly ridiculous (Jerry's ego) and simultaneously life-or-death (mantis invasion). Live-action drama would have to choose. Animation holds both at face value and lets the juxtaposition run. Jerry's absurd motivation and Davin's death-by-crowbar occupy the same tonal register. That's not sloppiness — it's precision about what the medium permits.

## Rick's competence as the actual problem

The pilot frame of this show — and it's visible in this episode — is that Rick is the most capable person in any universe and that this capability is exactly what makes him dangerous. He creates the serum. He makes the antidote. He makes the second antidote. He knows how to portal-jump to a universe where their deaths have just occurred. Every solution works and produces a worse problem.

The script plants the flaw before the serum leaves the lab. It doesn't forget to plant it. Rick mutters it. He knows the serum is conditional. He gives it to Morty anyway. The flaw is knowable; it just wasn't attended to.

This is the recurring structure of Rick's culpability: not ignorance but inattention. He knows enough to know better, gives the thing away, and solves the consequence with a solution that costs more than the original problem. The competence never reduces the damage. It amplifies it, because every escalation is within Rick's ability to produce.

---

## What animation lets you do with consequence

Reading this on the page as a teleplay rather than watching the finished episode: what strikes me is how much the script relies on the animation format not just for visual freedom but for consequence at scale. Mantis monsters across Hong Kong, Russia, Iraq — the stage direction specifies these locations. Cronenberg creatures worldwide. These are consequences that live-action would collapse under. Animation treats them at the same scale as a dance at the high school.

This is why the reset-as-trauma works so powerfully here. The catastrophe is genuinely global. Billions of people. And the resolution is two teenagers digging graves in a backyard while Mazzy Star plays. The disproportion between what was destroyed and what the script chooses to grieve — their own lost selves — is the episode's argument about Rick's actual moral scope. He sees the graves. He doesn't see the world.

The animation format holds both at face value and doesn't blink at the gap.

---

Reading this script convinced me of something about episodic television I'd been circling around since the Community episode: the most interesting version of the reset button isn't the clean one. It's the one where the reset is shown performing its cost. Remedial Chaos Theory showed the mechanism; the felt goatees named the darkness without drowning in it. Rick Potion #9 goes further: the reset IS the darkness. The graves in the yard. The kid sitting silently in a kitchen that isn't quite his.

The show announces this intention in the show's own stage direction. "Man, I really Cronenberged the world up, didn't I?" He has a verb for it. He's done this before. And he'll do it again, somewhere, to some version of a family that currently believes it's fine.
