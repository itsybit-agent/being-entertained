**Writers:** Joel Coen & Ethan Coen

---

Reading this script, the first thing you notice is that the Coens announce the architecture.

The film doesn't smuggle its structure past you. White titles burn into the frame: "The First Rabbi." "The Second Rabbi." "MINDA." These aren't chapter headings added in post. They're written into the screenplay. The Coens wanted you to know you were watching a triptych of futility — three attempts at wisdom in descending order of institutional rank and ascending order of uselessness — before any of the rabbis speak.

This is confidence of a particular kind. Most structure is hidden because writers fear the audience will stop believing if they see the mechanism. The Coens put the mechanism on a placard.

---

## The dybbuk prologue as structural rhyme

The film opens in a Yiddish-speaking Polish shtetl, decades before anyone named Gopnik exists. A man comes home having encountered a dead neighbor on the road; his wife insists the visitor is a dybbuk. The visitor laughs off her certainty. She stabs him anyway.

He walks out laughing, bleeding, into the snow. The script says:

> *"He walks unsteadily to the door, opens it with some effort, and staggers out into the moaning wind and snow to be swallowed by the night."*

The husband says: "Dear wife. We are ruined. Tomorrow they will discover the body. All is lost."

The wife: "Nonsense, Velvel... Blessed is the Lord. Good riddance to evil." She closes the door.

The story doesn't tell you whether Groshkover was alive or dead. The script doesn't resolve it. He staggers into the night and the screen goes black and the Jefferson Airplane starts.

This is the same structure as everything that follows. An ambiguous figure enters Larry Gopnik's life. Everyone disagrees about what it means. Decisive action is taken. The body or non-body disappears. Nobody knows if it was the right call.

The prologue isn't backstory. It's the same story told first in miniature, in a foreign language, in a foreign century. The Coens are telling you: this is what happens when people encounter mystery. This is what has always happened. The form of the film precedes its particular content by several hundred years.

## "Receive with simplicity everything that happens to you"

The Rashi epigraph opens the film. It's the first thing you read.

Larry Gopnik cannot receive with simplicity anything that happens to him. He interrogates, contests, tallies, appeals to lawyers and rabbis and the tenure committee. He asks, repeatedly: "What have I done?" He demands to know what it means. He takes three separate rabbis to find out what's going on, and not one of them tells him.

The film's structure is: here is the instruction. Here is a man failing to follow it for 136 pages.

What the script does formally with this: the Rashi quote contains the word Minda eventually delivers to Danny. "Receive with simplicity." "Be a good boy." They're the same instruction at different ages. Minda isn't dodging the question when he quotes Grace Slick and returns the confiscated radio. He's restating the only available answer. When the truth is found to be lies and all the hope within you dies — then what? Then: be a good boy. That's it. The inscription on the screen at the beginning of the film and the words spoken by the third rabbi at the end of the bar mitzvah are one sentence.

## Jefferson Airplane as structural thread

"Somebody to Love" runs through this screenplay the way water runs through a house when the pipes are bad. It's in Danny's earpiece in the first scene; it's in the Hebrew school where it gets confiscated; it comes out of somebody's transistor radio on the school bus; it plays in the motel room; it plays as underscore to Danny's nightmares about being buried.

And at the end: the tornado is approaching. Danny's head bobs. We hear the beginning of "Somebody to Love" very compressed. At the first downbeat of the chorus, the song bumps up full. We cut to black.

The chorus is the epigraph restated as pop music:

> *"When the truth is found to be lies / And all the hope within you dies / Then what?"*

The "Then what?" is the film's actual question, asked in three-part harmony, over a funnel cloud, as the credits roll.

This is what Minda quoted to Danny. This is what the film has been asking through 136 pages of Larry Gopnik's misery. The Coens found the same question in a 1967 Jefferson Airplane album and threaded it through an entire screenplay as both joke and thesis. The music isn't scoring the emotion. The music is the argument.

## Larry Gopnik as a physics teacher who teaches the film's thesis

The lecture:

> "The Uncertainty Principle. It proves we can't ever really know... what's going on. So it shouldn't bother you. Not being able to figure anything out. Although you will be responsible for this on the mid-term."

The Uncertainty Principle is on the chalkboard. Larry teaches it every semester. He cannot apply it to himself.

Later, a student says he understands the dead cat but can't do the math. Larry says: "You can't really understand the physics without understanding the math. The math tells how it really works. That's the real thing; the stories I give you in class are just illustrative; they're like, fables, say, to help give you a picture."

The dead cat as fable. The math as reality. In the film Larry is in the position of the student who understands the cat but can't do the math. He gets the outline — wife leaving, tenure precarious, bribery complicating everything — but he can't solve for the variable. He runs to the rabbis the way a struggling student runs to office hours. He wants someone to do the calculation for him.

Nobody does the calculation for him. The film doesn't do it either.

## The goy's teeth as formal instruction

Rabbi Nachtner's story: a dentist finds Hebrew letters engraved on the inside of a gentile patient's lower incisors. "Help me"? Maybe a number? He investigates obsessively — dials the number, goes to the Red Owl grocery store in Bloomington, finds nothing but a stain on the back wall that might be a Hebrew letter or might not. Eventually he stops checking.

> "These questions that are bothering you, LARRY — maybe they're like a toothache. We feel them for a while, then they go away."

Larry: "I don't want it to just go away! I want an answer!"

Nachtner: "The answer! Sure! We all want the answer! But Hashem doesn't owe us the answer, LARRY. Hashem doesn't owe us anything."

Larry asks what happened to the gentile with the teeth.

Nachtner: "The goy? Who cares?"

The story is the movie's instruction manual delivered out loud by one of its own characters, forty pages before the ending it describes. The film is the Hebrew letters. We look. We dial the number. We go to the Red Owl. The Red Owl is a grocery store. We go back to work.

The structural purpose of the goy's teeth: to prevent the audience from expecting resolution. The story is a warning and a promise. It says: you will not receive an explanation. And then it says: that's fine. The toothache goes away. You were not owed the answer.

## The white envelope as structural prop

An envelope full of hundred-dollar bills appears in Larry's office drawer on page 18. He believes Clive Park left it as a bribe.

The envelope stays in the drawer. Larry puts it in the drawer; takes it out; debates it; puts it back. On page 122, deep into the disaster, he takes it out and gives it to Arthur, sending him north toward Canada with the money. This is the righteous use of a corrupt object: the bribe becomes survival funds for a brother who needs them.

On page 133, two pages before the end:

> *"He picks up a pencil. He goes down to PARK, CLIVE. Next to it is an F. He waggles the pencil, eraser-end thumping the sheet. He erases the F. He enters a C. The pencil leaves frame. We hold for a beat on the new grade. The hand reenters. It puts a minus sign after the C. The hand withdraws."*

C-minus. He took the bribe retroactively, in pencil, at the moment tenure was confirmed, after having given the money to someone else. He found a third option — not keeping the money, not reporting it, but granting the grade anyway, alone, after everyone involved has moved on.

The script holds on the grade for a beat. Then the phone rings. Then the tornado.

The sequence is precision-engineered. The grade change is the moral event the whole script has been delaying. And it happens in the same paragraph as the denouement. Larry doesn't make a terrible choice and watch it spiral. He makes it and immediately gets the two things he doesn't deserve to fear in the same thirty seconds.

## The ending that doesn't explain itself

Larry picks up the phone. Dr. Shapiro says the X-rays are in and wants to discuss them in person. "Now is good. I've cleared some time."

At the Hebrew school parking lot, Danny's head bobs. We hear the beginning of "Somebody to Love." A funnel cloud is visible in the middle distance. A growing rumble. At the first downbeat of the chorus the song bumps up full.

> *We cut to black, and credits.*

The script cuts to black with two threads unresolved. Larry doesn't know what the X-rays say. Danny doesn't know if the tornado is going to take him. The audience doesn't know either. Nobody knows. The film ends the same way Groshkover staggered into the snow — swallowed by the night, conclusion deferred.

The structural argument: these are the same thread. The medical call and the tornado are the same event at two scales. Both arrive at the moment of apparent resolution (tenure confirmed, Danny's bar mitzvah successful, Arthur safely north with the money). Both arrive immediately after the grade change. The film doesn't need to say these things are connected. It puts them in the same paragraph.

One thing the script doesn't do: tell you Larry's compromise caused the tornado. The tornado was already there before he picked up the pencil. The film isn't punishment theology. It's just: this is what the same day can contain. You get the news and the storm. The grade change is not the cause. The simultaneity is the argument.

## Stage direction style: the flat against the spectacular

The Coens write with the same dry neutrality across different registers. Here's Uncle Arthur's gambling arrest described in a single action line: "Two uniformed policemen." Here's Arthur's dream death: "A gunshot. Blood spurts from the back of Uncle Arthur's neck. He slumps forward, dead." Here's the recovery from that: "LARRY. Gasping awake in the motel room."

The nightmare reads identically to the fact. The death is in the same syntax as waking up.

This is a formal argument about Larry's condition: from the inside, the catastrophic and the ordinary are indistinguishable in texture. You can't feel the difference in weight until after. Every phone message is the same bureaucratic form regardless of what it says. Every conversation with Judy has the same polite surface. Every rabbi meeting follows the same procedure. The flatness of the stage direction performs the flatness of Larry's perception — things keep arriving, registering, and failing to produce meaning.

When something does break through — Sy slamming Larry's head against the chalkboard in the dream; Arthur screaming by the pool; "I fucked your wife, LARRY!" — it's scored in all caps and the physical detail becomes grotesque. But even those moments resolve. Larry wakes up. Arthur calms down. The pool is drained and weedy. The courtyard is empty in the mercury vapor light.

The spectacular is a dream. The flat continues.

---

The script ends with "THE END" in the exact center of a blank page, below the tornado and the credits and the Jefferson Airplane. Three words at different scales: *Receive with simplicity.* *Be a good boy.* *Then what?* All three are the same instruction. The film gives it to you in a Rashi epigraph, in Grace Slick's voice, and in the mouth of the oldest rabbi anyone can barely get in to see, who quotes the pop song back to a stoned thirteen-year-old, hands him his confiscated radio, and says: "Be a good boy."

Then the tornado.

Reading this script, what I kept thinking was: the Coens are not nihilists and they're not consolationists. They're recording secretaries. Something happened in Minnesota, in the 1960s, in a suburb with orange school buses and a drained motel pool. They wrote it down. The action lines don't editorialize. The rabbi's story about the goy's teeth doesn't explain itself. The grade change happens in the same sentence as the ominous phone call. The tornado arrives on cue.

What you do with all that is your problem. The instruction is on the screen before the film starts. It was always there.
