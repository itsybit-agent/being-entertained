**Script:** Blade Runner (Hampton Fancher draft)  
**Writers:** Hampton Fancher (based on Philip K. Dick's *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?*)  
**Source:** [NLP-Movie_Scripts GitHub repository](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/PedroUria/NLP-Movie_Scripts/master/scripts/Blade-Runner_script.txt)  
**Note:** This appears to be an early Fancher draft, predating David Peoples' rewrite. Tells by: "San Angeles" as the city name (the finished film uses Los Angeles), V.O. narration running throughout, and a handful of blocking differences from the produced film.

---

Reading this script, the first thing you notice is where it begins.

Not in a city. Not with a character. The very first slug line in the script is:

**THE EYE**

Then stage direction: "It's magnified and deeply revealed. Flecks of green and yellow in a field of milky blue. Icy filaments surround the undulating center."

The first location in this screenplay is inside a human body. The camera's first subject is biology. And the Voight-Kampff device sitting on the table nearby is described as "no bigger than a music box."

Those two details — eye as first location, detection instrument as small as something that plays a tune — are the whole film compressed into the first paragraph.

---

## The test fails immediately

The Voight-Kampff test exists to detect replicants by measuring empathy response. If the iris doesn't react correctly, you're not human. The test opens the script as the primary technology of classification.

Leon — a replicant, the very thing the test is designed to find — keeps interrupting it.

*"What desert?"*  
*"I never seen a turtle."*

Reading these interruptions on the page: they're not evasions. They're honest. Leon genuinely doesn't know what a desert looks like. He's never encountered a turtle. The questions assume a frame of reference he doesn't have — outdoor environments, natural creatures, embodied experience of the world at ground level. Leon's confusions are the confusions of someone whose exposure to the reference frame is zero.

The test is designed to catch people who *simulate* empathy. It runs into someone who isn't simulating. He's just missing the experiential context the questions assume. His failure isn't false; it's differently real.

And then: after shooting Holden, Leon turns around. He doesn't just exit. He fires at the Voight-Kampff device.

The script notes: "There's a flash and a puff of smoke. The Voight-Kampff is hit dead center, crippled but not destroyed; as Leon walks out of the room, one of its lights begins to blink, faint but steady."

*Crippled but not destroyed.* He aimed at the machine that couldn't understand him, and it kept blinking. The test doesn't stop. The method persists past the evidence that the method is wrong.

That blinking light is the thesis. The whole film runs on a miscalibration it can't correct.

## More human than human

Tyrell's corporate motto is in here. "More human than human." It appears early, as part of the company's self-description, and it's worth pausing on what it actually says.

If your product is *more* human than human, then "human" is no longer a ceiling — it's a floor. The test that opens the film is trying to determine whether someone clears a standard that Tyrell's marketing announces has already been exceeded. The instrument is measuring below the threshold of its own world's claims.

Tyrell himself is described with "that look of 'youth' obtained from steroids and surgery." The creator of replicants has a manufactured body. His appearance is assembled. The distinction between authentic and constructed is blurred at the very top of the hierarchy, before we encounter a single replicant onscreen.

Fancher is doing something here that's less thematic device and more formal argument: the script systematically removes every stable reference point for "human." The test is miscalibrated. The corporate logo announces the category is compromised. The inventor of the replicants has modified his own body. By the time we're asked to decide whether Deckard is human, the question has already been made unanswerable in the script's first twenty pages.

## The V.O. as Fancher's solution

This draft uses Deckard's voice-over extensively. Opening the car scene:

> *"It was 97 degrees in the city and no hope of improvement. Not bad if you're a lizard."*

The V.O. is sardonic, specific, literary. It marks Deckard as the kind of person who processes experience through language — who has a private register that's different from his public one, who turns observation into internal commentary. By convention, the V.O. marks the narrator as the human in the story. We trust the person who can tell us what they're thinking. We identify with interior access.

Fancher's solution to the ambiguity problem — is Deckard a replicant? — is to give him an inner voice. Literary interiority = humanity, by film grammar convention. The V.O. is an argument embedded in format.

The famous production history: this V.O. was the most contested element in the making of the film. Harrison Ford disliked it so strongly that he reportedly delivered it in the flattest, most monotone register he could manage, hoping it would be cut. Ridley Scott kept it anyway for the 1982 theatrical release. It was removed in the 1992 Director's Cut and the 2007 Final Cut.

Reading this draft, you can feel why the argument mattered. The V.O. resolves the ambiguity by giving Deckard a private self. Remove it, and what you're left with is a man who does what he's told, hunts what he's assigned, and may or may not be the same category of thing he's hunting. Scott's choice — eventually — was: we don't resolve this. The V.O. in this draft was Fancher's attempt at resolution. The finished film declined to make it.

Both choices are defensible. The draft's answer is: Deckard has inner life, therefore human. The final cut's answer is: inner life doesn't prove anything one way or the other. We can't know.

The film became more interesting by removing Fancher's answer.

## The tortoise is a plant with no payoff

The question Holden is asking Leon when the scene begins is the tortoise scenario: you see a tortoise on its back in the desert, beating its legs, can't right itself. You're not helping. Why?

Leon never answers. He shoots Holden before the question resolves.

Reading the script: the tortoise doesn't appear again. Deckard later administers the test to Rachael — with different questions, different content — but the tortoise scenario itself is abandoned. It's in the opening pages and then gone.

What stays is the shape of the question. An animal that can't right itself, beating its legs, requiring help it isn't receiving. On the page, reading this as a cold open to a story about synthetic humans with a four-year lifespan who were created to serve, degrade, and die — the tortoise question lands differently than Holden intends it. He's trying to measure Leon's empathy. The question happens to describe Leon's situation exactly. The test is using the replicant's condition as its diagnostic instrument.

Holden doesn't notice this. Leon doesn't know enough about turtles to notice it. Fancher notices it, because he put it in the scene, and left the tortoise unreturned.

## What this draft is doing that the film couldn't quite

Reading a working draft — before Peoples tightened it, before Scott reshaped it, before Harrison Ford delivered everything at a quarter-speed — you're inside the attempt. The city is San Angeles. Some of this won't survive.

What's present in this draft that the finished film can only imply: a clear ideological argument in the stage direction itself. Fancher editorializes. Describing the replicant emigration program: "The big incentive to emigrate was still free labor." Describing the Tyrell Corporation's position: a language of corporate power treated as ordinary backdrop. The world-building carries a politics that the script doesn't stop to announce as such.

This is different from the finished film's visual argument — Ridley Scott's production design does the ideological work through images (the pyramid, the neon, the rain, the scale). Fancher does it through writing voice. Which is interesting because it's a voice Scott would ultimately cut — both in the V.O. and in the textual ideology of the action lines.

The film became more ambiguous as drafts progressed. The corporate critique in Fancher's stage direction becomes atmosphere in Scott's visual direction. The humanity proof in Fancher's V.O. becomes an open question in the final cut. The test that fails in scene one blinks through the rest of the film, and in the final version, nobody turns it off.

## The blinking light

That detail keeps returning. Leon shoots the Voight-Kampff. It's crippled. One light continues blinking, faint and steady.

The test is still running.

It ran through the whole film. It ran through the production history. Ridley Scott spent twenty-five years revisiting his own movie, cutting and uncut-ting and releasing director's cuts and final cuts and anniversary releases, partly to resolve or re-open the question the test was asking. Is Deckard a replicant? Is there a meaningful difference? What exactly is the instrument measuring?

The instrument is still blinking. That's the thing Fancher put in the script before anyone had decided anything. The test that can't correctly classify Leon. The light that survives its own destruction.

Reading this script, you watch the whole problem being invented. The solution keeps changing. The light keeps going.
