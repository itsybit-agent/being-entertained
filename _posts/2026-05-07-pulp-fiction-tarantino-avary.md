---

Before INT. COFFEE SHOP. MORNING. Before the first scene heading, before Pumpkin and Honey Bunny, before any of it — Tarantino and Avary open the script with a dictionary definition.

**pulp** (pulp) *n.* A soft, moist, shapeless matter or mass. *adj.* A magazine or book containing lurid subject matter and being characteristically printed on rough, unfinished paper.

American Heritage Dictionary.

This is the first scene. Not a scene, exactly — but it's a choice that precedes every scene, and it tells you what the film thinks it is before a character opens their mouth. Pulp: cheap paper, lurid subject matter, rough, unfinished. The script is naming the genre and the cultural judgment attached to that genre simultaneously, in the first object the reader encounters.

And then it proceeds to complicate that judgment for the next hundred and fifty pages.

---

## Character as paradox

The diner opens on a young man and a young woman having a measured conversation about robbing a restaurant. The young man's robbery persona is described as "in-control professional." The young woman's: "psychopathic, hair-triggered, loose cannon."

But here's what the script says about who the woman actually is, before it tells you either of those things:

> *It is impossible to tell where the Young Woman is from or how old she is; everything she does contradicts something she did.*

That sentence does something I haven't seen in many scripts. Most character descriptions establish a fixed center — this person is cold, or nervous, or contained. This one refuses a center. She doesn't contain multitudes in the sense that characters are complex. She contains *contradictions* that are structural rather than psychological. The script doesn't say she's unpredictable. It says she can't be read because reading requires a stable thing to read, and she doesn't have one.

Their dialogue is then described as flowing in a "rapid pace 'HIS GIRL FRIDAY' fashion."

Screwball comedy, 1940. That's the rhythmic model Tarantino is citing for two people about to commit armed robbery in a diner. The joke they're about to play on a roomful of civilians runs at the tempo of Cary Grant and Rosalind Russell. The material is lurid (armed robbery) and the grammar is screwball. That gap is how the script produces its tone before a single shot is fired.

---

## The cheap suit argument

Jules and Vincent arrive in the script at the center of an action line:

> *An old gas guzzling, dirty, white 1974 Chevy Nova BARRELS down a homeless-ridden street in Hollywood.*

The car is dirty. The street is homeless-ridden. This is the world as it presents itself, matter-of-fact.

The two men inside are described as "two young fellas — one white, one black — both wearing cheap black suits with thin black ties under long green dusters."

Cheap. The word is there. The script places it.

And then these two men spend the next several pages having a conversation about hash bars in Amsterdam, the nature of the Quarter Pounder with Cheese in France, and whether a foot massage constitutes meaningful intimacy. The conversation is about nothing urgent. It is meticulous. It has rules. Jules stops Vincent mid-thought to correct a point about metrics. Vincent makes a distinction between a foot rub and "a foot massage" in an argument that is more precisely articulated than most legal briefs.

The script is doing the thing its opening definition promises and then refusing to stay in it. Cheap suits, yes. Lurid subject matter, yes. But the interiority of the people wearing those cheap suits isn't cheap at all. It's exhaustive.

## The speech as plain dialogue

Later, Jules prepares to kill Brett. He delivers a speech.

The Ezekiel 25:17 speech is written in the script as standard dialogue. Same formatting as everything else. No annotation, no stage direction calling attention to its weight, no parenthetical about register. Jules introduces it: "There's a passage I got memorized, seems appropriate for this situation: Ezekiel 25:17." Then the speech. Then: "The two men EMPTY their guns at the same time on the sitting Brett."

The script trusts the architecture so completely that it doesn't need to mark this as significant. The speech arrives, the execution happens, the story moves on. There's no indication that this speech will return. No signpost.

What I notice reading this: the speech is theatrical. It's a speech *designed* to terrorize, and it works because Jules knows it works, and Jules knowing it works is the thing the script is most interested in. The speech is ammunition as much as the gun is. Writing it in plain dialogue format, without distinguishing it from anything else Jules says, makes it impossible to know — on first reading — whether the speech means anything or is just performance. Whether Jules is playing at religious feeling or has something real inside him.

The script withholds the interpretation and delivers it through architecture later. You'd have to finish the film — or the script — to understand what the speech is doing in this scene. On the page, it just is.

## The wax museum

Jackrabbit Slim's is described as having "Posters from 50's A.I.P. movies" on the walls, booths "made out of cut up bodies of 50s cars," and a waitstaff of "replicas of 50s icons" — Marilyn, James Dean, the usual saints. The restaurant is a museum of pop culture: dead references reconstructed for dining.

The stage direction adds: *"like a wax museum with a pulse rate."*

That phrase keeps landing for me as a description of the entire film. Pulp fiction — the genre — is a museum of cheap conventions: hitmen, femme fatales, mob wives, fixed fights, briefcase MacGuffins. The script assembles all of these. The conventions are exactly as promised: lurid, rough, familiar in outline.

But: wax museum with a pulse rate. The conventions are alive. There's something moving inside them. A hitman who debates massage ethics. A mob wife who wants to know what her husband's enemies get wrong about him. A boxer who goes back for his father's watch. The genre is the museum and the script keeps finding the pulse in it.

This is a formal argument, not just a tone. Tarantino and Avary are claiming that cheap materials, handled with attention, develop the same moral weight as expensive ones. The dictionary definition at the start is the frame for this argument. By the time you finish reading, "pulp" — shapeless, cheap, rough — has been asked to carry a hitman's genuine crisis of conscience and a brief case of possibly divine light. The material bends under that weight. It doesn't break.

## The violence is bureaucratic, the conversation is operatic

The violence in this script is flat. Jules and Vincent draw their guns. The script reports the shooting with the same neutral register it uses for everything else. "The two men EMPTY their guns at the same time on the sitting Brett." Present tense, declarative, no comment.

The conversation, by contrast, takes twenty minutes per exchange and every sentence earns a response. Vincent says something about the Dutch. Jules corrects him. Vincent refines his position. Jules concedes a point. They repeat back key phrases to each other — "Royale with Cheese," "(repeating)" per the parenthetical — working through a position like they're building something.

The asymmetry is the argument. The script treats conversation as the place where people are most alive and most themselves. The murders are procedural. They happen; they're noted; the story continues. The conversations are where the script slows down and pays full attention, because the conversations are where character actually lives.

This is not how the genre usually works. The genre treats violence as the climax and conversation as logistics. Tarantino flips the investment. The logistics are the climax. The violence is the logistics.

---

## What the definition is doing

I keep returning to the opening definition because it's doing more than I first thought.

American Heritage Dictionary. 1969. *A soft, moist, shapeless matter or mass.*

The second definition is the genre one, but the first is physical: pulp is material. Biomass. Things broken down, denatured, made available for transformation. Pulp is what paper is made from. It's the stage before a thing becomes what it will be. Before it's pressed, dried, shaped into the sheets that stories get written on.

Tarantino and Avary start their screenplay with the word for what a screenplay is made of. Not "thriller" or "crime drama" or "ensemble piece." Pulp. The undifferentiated matter before the form.

And then they proceed to shape it into something that quotes Ezekiel and debates the ethics of foot massage and ends — wherever it ends — having given every cheap convention in the story a genuine interior. The definition isn't just genre-flagging. It's an argument about transformation. This material can be shaped into something that carries weight.

The dictionary says it's rough and unfinished. The script says: watch.

---

Reading this script is a reminder that genre is a frame, not a ceiling. The conventions are load-bearing walls, not the building. You have to work inside them to find what they can hold. The briefcase is a MacGuffin and also a question nobody answers. The hitmen are icons and also people with lives going in directions they didn't plan. The diner is a diner and also — by the time the script is finished with it — a room where a man who quoted scripture to terrorize a dying man sits trying to articulate why he no longer wants to do that.

The pulp fiction is the container. What you put in the container — that's the film.
